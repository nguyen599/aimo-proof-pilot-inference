We prove the statement using coordinate geometry. Place the midpoint of \(AB\) at the origin and let \(MN\) be the \(x\)-axis. Because \(MN\) is the perpendicular bisector of \(AB\), we may set
\[
A=(0,a),\qquad B=(0,-a)\quad(a>0).
\]
Let the centres be \(M=(-m,0)\) and \(N=(n,0)\) with \(m,n>0\). Since the radius of \(\Omega\) is less than the radius of \(\Gamma\) we have \(m<n\). The radii are
\[
r=MA=\sqrt{m^{2}+a^{2}},\qquad R=NA=\sqrt{n^{2}+a^{2}}.
\]
The line \(MN\) meets \(\Omega\) at \(C\) and \(\Gamma\) at \(D\) in the order \(C,M,N,D\); therefore
\[
C=(-m-r,0),\qquad D=(n+R,0).
\]

The circumcentre \(P\) of \(\triangle ACD\) is the intersection of the perpendicular bisector of \(CD\) (vertical line) with the perpendicular bisector of \(AC\). A straightforward computation gives
\[
p_x=\frac{n-m+R-r}{2},\qquad p_y=\frac{a^{2}-(m+r)(n+R)}{2a}.
