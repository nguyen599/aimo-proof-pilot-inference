## Problem
Let $\Omega$ and $\Gamma$ be circles with centres $M$ and $N$, respectively, such that the radius of $\Omega$ is less than the radius of $\Gamma$. Suppose $\Omega$ and $\Gamma$ intersect at two distinct points $A$ and $B$. Line $MN$ intersects $\Omega$ at $C$ and $\Gamma$ at $D$, so that $C, M, N, D$ lie on $MN$ in that order. Let $P$ be the circumcentre of triangle $ACD$. Line $AP$ meets $\Omega$ again at $E\neq A$ and meets $\Gamma$ again at $F\neq A$. Let $H$ be the orthocentre of triangle $PMN$.

Prove that the line through $H$ parallel to $AP$ is tangent to the circumcircle of triangle $BEF$.

## Solution
We prove the statement using analytic geometry.  
By similarity we may place the circles conveniently.

**1. Coordinate set‑up.**  
Place the centre \(M\) of \(\Omega\) at \((0,0)\) and the centre \(N\) of \(\Gamma\) at \((1,0)\).  
Let \(\Omega\) be the unit circle: \(x^2+y^2=1\).  
Let the two intersection points be \(A=(a,b)\) with \(b>0\) and \(B=(a,-b)\).  
Hence  
\[
a^2+b^2=1. \tag{1}
\]  
The centre \(N=(1,0)\) is the centre of \(\Gamma\), so  
\[
(a-1)^2+b^2 = R^2. \tag{2}
\]  
From (1) and (2) we obtain \(R^2=2-2a\).  
Because the radius of \(\Omega\) is less than that of \(\Gamma\) we have \(R>1\) and consequently \(a<\frac12\).  
The line \(MN\) is the \(x\)-axis. Its second intersection with \(\Omega\) is \(C=(-1,0)\) and with \(\Gamma\) is \(D=(1+R,0)\).  
The order \(C,M,N,D\) on the \(x\)-axis is as required.

**2. Trigonometric parametrisation.**  
Since \(a\in(-1,\frac12)\) and \(b>0\), we can set  
\[
a = \cos 2\alpha,\quad b = \sin 2\alpha\qquad\text{for some }\alpha\in(\tfrac\pi6,\tfrac\pi2).
\]  
Then \(R^2 = 2-2\cos2\alpha = 4\sin^2\alpha\), so \(R = 2\sin\alpha\).  
Thus  
\[
C=(-1,0),\quad D=(1+2\sin\alpha,0),\quad M=(0,0),\quad N=(1,0).
\]

**3. The point \(P\).**  
\(P\) is the circumcentre of \(\triangle ACD\).  
Because \(C\) and \(D\) lie on the \(x\)-axis, the perpendicular bisector of \(CD\) is the vertical line  
\[
x = \frac{-1+(1+2\sin\alpha)}{2} = \sin\alpha.
\]  
Hence \(P\) has coordinates \((\sin\alpha,\;y_P)\).  
Using \(|P-C|=|P-A|\) we get  
\[
(\sin\alpha+1)^2 + y_P^2 = (\sin\alpha-\cos2\alpha)^2 + (y_P-\sin2\alpha)^2.
\]  
Simplifying yields \(y_P = -\cos\alpha\). Therefore  
\[
P = (\sin\alpha,\;-\cos\alpha).
\]  
Note that \(P\) lies on \(\Omega\) because \(\sin^2\alpha+\cos^2\alpha=1\).

**4. The points \(E\) and \(F\).**  
The line \(AP\) is parametrised by \(z = A + t\mathbf v\) with \(\mathbf v = P-A\).  
Compute  
\[
\mathbf v = (\sin\alpha-\cos2\alpha,\; -\cos\alpha-\sin2\alpha).
\]  
Using the double‑angle formulas,
\[
\sin\alpha-\cos2\alpha = \sin\alpha-(1-2\sin^2\alpha) = 2\sin^2\alpha+\sin\alpha-1 = (2\sin\alpha-1)(\sin\alpha+1),
\]  
\[
-\cos\alpha-\sin2\alpha = -\cos\alpha-2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha = -\cos\alpha(2\sin\alpha+1).
\]  
Now \(|\mathbf v|^2 = |P-A|^2\). Because \(A\) and \(P\) are both on \(\Omega\),
\[
|\mathbf v|^2 = 2-2P\!\cdot\!A.
\]  
Since \(P\!\cdot\!A = \sin\alpha\cos2\alpha -\cos\alpha\sin2\alpha = \sin(\alpha-2\alpha) = -\sin\alpha\),
\[
|\mathbf v|^2 = 2+2\sin\alpha = 2(1+\sin\alpha). \tag{3}
\]

*Intersection with \(\Omega\).*  
\(A\) is on \(\Omega\), so \(|A+t\mathbf v|^2=1\) gives \(2t(A\!\cdot\!\mathbf v)+t^2|\mathbf v|^2=0\).  
Thus the second intersection \(E\) corresponds to  
\[
t_E = -\frac{2A\!\cdot\!\mathbf v}{|\mathbf v|^2}.
\]  
We have \(A\!\cdot\!\mathbf v = A\!\cdot\!(P-A) = A\!\cdot\!P - |A|^2 = -\sin\alpha-1\).  
Hence  
\[
t_E = -\frac{2(-\sin\alpha-1)}{2(1+\sin\alpha)} = 1,
\]  
so \(E = A + \mathbf v = P\). Therefore \(E = P\).

*Intersection with \(\Gamma\).*  
\(A\) is on \(\Gamma\), so \(|(A+t\mathbf v)-N|^2=R^2\) gives \(2t((A-N)\!\cdot\!\mathbf v)+t^2|\mathbf v|^2=0\).  
Thus the second intersection \(F\) corresponds to  
\[
t_F = -\frac{2(A-N)\!\cdot\!\mathbf v}{|\mathbf v|^2}.
\]  
Compute \((A-N)\!\cdot\!\mathbf v = (A-N)\!\cdot\!(P-A) = (A-N)\!\cdot\!P - (A-N)\!\cdot\!A\).  
Now \((A-N)\!\cdot\!P = (\cos2\alpha-1)\sin\alpha + \sin2\alpha(-\cos\alpha) = -2\sin^2\alpha\sin\alpha -2\sin\alpha\cos^2\alpha = -2\sin\alpha\).  
Also \((A-N)\!\cdot\!A = |A|^2 - N\!\cdot\!A = 1-\cos2\alpha\).  
Hence  
\[
(A-N)\!\cdot\!\mathbf v = -2\sin\alpha - (1-\cos2\alpha) = -2\sin\alpha -1 + \cos2\alpha.
\]  
Using \(\cos2\alpha = 1-2\sin^2\alpha\), this simplifies to  
\[
-2\sin\alpha -1 +1-2\sin^2\alpha = -2\sin\alpha(1+\sin\alpha).
\]  
Therefore  
\[
t_F = -\frac{2(-2\sin\alpha(1+\sin\alpha))}{2(1+\sin\alpha)} = 2\sin\alpha.
\]  
So  
\[
F = A + 2\sin\alpha\,\mathbf v = (1-2\sin\alpha)A + 2\sin\alpha P.
\]  
With \(s = \sin\alpha\), \(c = \cos\alpha\) we obtain  
\[
F = \bigl(1-2s+4s^3,\; -4s^2c\bigr). \tag{4}
\]

**5. Orthocentre \(H\) of \(\triangle PMN\).**  
\(M=(0,0)\), \(N=(1,0)\), \(P=(s,-c)\).  
Altitude from \(P\) is the vertical line \(x=s\).  
Altitude from \(M\) is perpendicular to \(PN\).  
\(PN = (1-s,\;c)\). Its slope is \(\frac{c}{1-s}\); the perpendicular slope is \(-\frac{1-s}{c}\).  
The equation through \(M\) is \(y = -\frac{1-s}{c}x\).  
Intersection with \(x=s\) gives  
\[
H_y = -\frac{1-s}{c}\,s = -\frac{s(1-s)}{c}.
\]  
Hence  
\[
H = \Bigl(s,\;-\frac{s(1-s)}{c}\Bigr). \tag{5}
\]

**6. Circumcentre \(O\) of \(\triangle BEF\).**  
Since \(E=P\), we have \(B,P,F\).  
Because \(B\) and \(P\) lie on \(\Omega\) (unit circle centred at \(M\)), the perpendicular bisector of \(BP\) passes through \(M\). Thus \(O\) satisfies  
\[
(P-B)\!\cdot\!O = 0. \tag{6}
\]  
Also \(|O-B|^2 = |O-F|^2\) gives  
\[
(F-B)\!\cdot\!O = \frac{|F|^2-|B|^2}{2}. \tag{7}
\]

\(B = (\cos2\alpha,\;-\sin2\alpha) = (1-2s^2,\;-2sc)\).  
So  
\[
P-B = \bigl(s-(1-2s^2),\; -c-(-2sc)\bigr) = \bigl(2s^2+s-1,\; c(2s-1)\bigr) = (2s-1)\,(s+1,\;c).
\]  
Thus (6) becomes  
\[
(s+1)x + c\,y = 0. \tag{8}
\]

Now compute \(F-B\) using (4).  
\(F = (1-2s+4s^3,\; -4s^2c)\).  
Hence  
\[
F-B = \bigl((1-2s+4s^3)-(1-2s^2),\; -4s^2c-(-2sc)\bigr) = \bigl(-2s+2s^2+4s^3,\; -2sc(2s-1)\bigr) = (2s-1)\,\bigl(2s(s+1),\; -2sc\bigr).
\]

Next compute \(|F|^2\).  
\[
|F|^2 = (1-2s+4s^3)^2 + (4s^2c)^2.
\]  
Using \(c^2=1-s^2\), we expand:
\[
(1-2s+4s^3)^2 = 1 -4s +4s^2 +8s^3 -16s^4 +16s^6,
\]  
\[
(4s^2c)^2 = 16s^4(1-s^2) = 16s^4 -16s^6.
\]  
Summing gives \(|F|^2 = 1 -4s +4s^2 +8s^3\).  
Since \(|B|^2 = 1\), the right‑hand side of (7) is  
\[
\frac{(1-4s+4s^2+8s^3)-1}{2} = \frac{-4s+4s^2+8s^3}{2} = -2s+2s^2+4s^3 = 2s(2s^2+s-1).
\]  
Now \(2s^2+s-1 = (2s-1)(s+1)\). Hence RHS \(= 2s(2s-1)(s+1)\).  

From (7) we have  
\[
(F-B)\!\cdot\!O = (2s-1)\bigl[2s(s+1)x - 2sc\,y\bigr] = 2s(2s-1)\bigl[(s+1)x - c\,y\bigr].
\]  
Setting this equal to \(2s(2s-1)(s+1)\) and cancelling the non‑zero factor \(2s(2s-1)\) (since \(s>0\) and \(2s-1>0\) by \(a<1/2\)), we obtain  
\[
(s+1)x - c\,y = s+1. \tag{9}
\]

Solving the linear system (8) and (9):
\[
\begin{cases}
(s+1)x + c y = 0,\\
(s+1)x - c y = s+1.
\end{cases}
\]  
Adding gives \(2(s+1)x = 2(s+1)\) ⇒ \(x = \frac12\).  
Substituting into (8): \(\frac12(s+1) + c y = 0\) ⇒ \(y = -\frac{s+1}{2c}\).  
Therefore  
\[
O = \Bigl(\frac12,\; -\frac{s+1}{2c}\Bigr). \tag{10}
\]

**7. Tangency of the line through \(H\) parallel to \(AP\).**  
The line \(\ell\) through \(H\) parallel to \(AP\) has direction \(\mathbf v = P-A\).  
Using the expressions for \(P\) and \(A\) we have  
\[
\mathbf v = \bigl((2s-1)(s+1),\; -c(2s+1)\bigr). \tag{11}
\]  
Hence \(\ell\) is parametrised by \(H + t\mathbf v\).

\[
O-H = \Bigl(\frac12 - s,\; -\frac{s+1}{2c} + \frac{s(1-s)}{c}\Bigr) = \Bigl(-\frac{2s-1}{2},\; \frac{ - (s+1) + 2s(1-s) }{2c}\Bigr) = \Bigl(-\frac{2s-1}{2},\; \frac{s-1-2s^2}{2c}\Bigr).
\]  
The distance from \(O\) to \(\ell\) is

d = \frac{|\det(O-H,\;\mathbf v)|}{|\mathbf v|}.
\]  
We calculate the determinant:
\[
\det(O-H,\mathbf v) = \Bigl(-\frac{2s-1}{2}\Bigr)\bigl(-c(2s+1)\bigr) - \Bigl(\frac{s-1-2s^2}{2c}\Bigr)\bigl((2s-1)(s+1)\bigr).
\]  
The first term is \(\frac{(2s-1)c(2s+1)}{2}\).  
The second term is \(\frac{(2s-1)(s+1)(s-1-2s^2)}{2c}\).

\]  
Multiply the bracket by \(2c\):

2c\det = (2s-1)\bigl[ c^2(2s+1) - (s+1)(s-1-2s^2) \bigr].
\]  
Now \(c^2 = 1-s^2\). Compute

c^2(2s+1) = (1-s^2)(2s+1) = 2s+1 -2s^3 - s^2,
\]  
\[
(s+1)(s-1-2s^2) = (s+1)(-2s^2+s-1) = -(2s^3+2s^2 - s^2 - s + s +1) = -(2s^3+s^2+1).
\]  
Thus
\[
c^2(2s+1) - (s+1)(s-1-2s^2) = (2s+1-2s^3-s^2) + (2s^3+s^2+1) = 2s+2 = 2(s+1).
\]  
Therefore
\[
2c\det = (2s-1)\cdot 2(s+1) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \det = \frac{(2s-1)(s+1)}{c}. \tag{12}
\]

Now \(|\mathbf v|^2 = 2(1+s)\) from (3), so \(|\mathbf v| = \sqrt{2(1+s)}\).  
Hence
\[
d = \frac{(2s-1)(s+1)}{c\sqrt{2(1+s)}} = \frac{(2s-1)\sqrt{s+1}}{c\sqrt{2}}. \tag{13}
\]

Next compute the circumradius \(R_0\) of \(\triangle BEF\) (i.e. of \(\triangle B P F\)).  
\[
O-B = \Bigl(\frac12 - (1-2s^2),\; -\frac{s+1}{2c} - (-2sc)\Bigr) = \Bigl(2s^2-\frac12,\; -\frac{s+1}{2c}+2sc\Bigr).
\]  
Write \(2s^2-\frac12 = \frac{4s^2-1}{2}\) and combine the \(y\)-component:

-\frac{s+1}{2c}+2sc = \frac{ - (s+1) + 4s c^2 }{2c} = \frac{4s(1-s^2) - s -1}{2c} = \frac{3s-4s^3-1}{2c}.
\]  
Thus
\[
R_0^2 = \Bigl(\frac{4s^2-1}{2}\Bigr)^2 + \Bigl(\frac{3s-4s^3-1}{2c}\Bigr)^2 = \frac{(4s^2-1)^2}{4} + \frac{(3s-4s^3-1)^2}{4c^2}.

Multiply by \(4c^2\):
\[
4c^2 R_0^2 = c^2(4s^2-1)^2 + (3s-4s^3-1)^2.
\]  
Expand the first term:
\[
c^2(4s^2-1)^2 = (1-s^2)(16s^4-8s^2+1) = -16s^6+24s^4-9s^2+1.
\]  
Expand the second term:

(3s-4s^3-1)^2 = 16s^6-24s^4+8s^3+9s^2-6s+1.
\]  
Sum:
\[
(-16s^6+24s^4-9s^2+1) + (16s^6-24s^4+8s^3+9s^2-6s+1) = 8s^3-6s+2.
\]  
Factor: \(8s^3-6s+2 = 2(4s^3-3s+1) = 2(2s-1)^2(s+1)\).  
Therefore
\[
4c^2 R_0^2 = 2(2s-1)^2(s+1) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad R_0^2 = \frac{(2s-1)^2(s+1)}{2c^2}.
\]  
But from (13) we have \(d^2 = \frac{(2s-1)^2(s+1)}{2c^2}\). Hence \(d = R_0\).  

Thus the distance from the circumcentre \(O\) of \(\triangle BEF\) to the line through \(H\) parallel to \(AP\) equals the circumradius \(R_0\), so the line is tangent to the circumcircle of \(\triangle BEF\). ∎
