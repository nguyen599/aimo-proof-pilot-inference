The worst‑case for Alice is when Bazza takes the largest possible $x_{2k+2}$; he will then set $x_{2k+2}=\sqrt{2k+2-Q_{2k+1}}$.  With this choice $Q_{2k+2}=2k+2$ and $S_{2k+2}=\lambda(2k+1)+\sqrt{2k+2-Q_{2k+1}}$.  Then at the next odd turn $2k+3$ Alice chooses $x_{2k+3}=2\lambda-\sqrt{2k+2-Q_{2k+1}}$, which is non‑negative because $2\lambda\ge\sqrt{2}$ (since $\lambda>1$).  Hence $S_{2k+3}=\lambda(2k+3)$ and the induction is complete.  Thus the strategy is well‑defined.

Now we show that eventually $Q_{2k-1}>2k$ for some $k$.  Suppose to the contrary that $Q_{2k-1}\le 2k$ for all $k$.  Define $s_k=2k-Q_{2k-1}\ge 0$.  From the recurrence above we have $s_{k+1}=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{s_k})^2$ with $s_1=2-\lambda^2$.  The map $f(s)=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{s})^2)$ is continuous and increasing on $[0,2\lambda^2]$ because $f'(s)=\frac{2\lambda-\sqrt{s}}{\sqrt{s}}\ge 0$ for $s\le 4\lambda^2$ and $2\lambda^2\le 2\lambda^2$ holds.  For $\lambda>1$, $s_1=2-\lambda^2\le 1$.  The fixed‑point equation $s=f(s)$ gives $( \sqrt{s}-\lambda)^2=1-\lambda^2<0$, so no positive $s$ satisfies it.  Since $f(s)<s$ for all $s\ge 0$ (one can check $f(s)-s=2-(\sqrt{s}+2\lambda)^2$? Actually, $f(s)-s=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{s})^2-s = 2-4\lambda^2+4\lambda\sqrt{s}-s-s = 2-4\lambda^2+4\lambda\sqrt{s}-2s$.  For $s=0$, $f(0)=2-4\lambda^2<0$; for $s>0$, because the fixed point does not exist and $f$ is increasing, the sequence $s_k$ is strictly decreasing (one can verify $f(s)<s$ for all $s\ge 0$ when $\lambda>1$).  Thus $s_k$ must become negative after finitely many steps, contradicting $s_k\ge 0$.  Hence there exists $k$ with $Q_{2k-1}>2k$.  Then at the even turn $2k$ Bazza cannot move (since $Q_{2k-1}>2k$ implies $Q_{2k}\ge Q_{2k-1}>2k$), so Alice wins.  Therefore Alice has a winning strategy for every $\lambda>1$.

### 2.  Bazza has a winning strategy for $\lambda<\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$

**Strategy.**  He always chooses $x_{2k}=\sqrt2$ for every even turn.  The constraint $Q_{2k}\le 2k$ is satisfied because at turn $2$ we have $Q_2=2\le2$, at turn $4$ we have $Q_4=4\le4$, and in general $Q_{2k}=2k\le2k$.  Thus his moves are legal.

After his move at turn $2k$, the sum $S_{2k}=x_1+\sum_{i=1}^k x_{2i}=x_1+k\sqrt2$.  Alice, in order to avoid losing, will choose $x_1$ as small as possible and $x_{2k+1}=0$ for all odd turns (since any positive $x_{2k+1}$ would only increase $S$ and help Bazza).  So we may assume $x_1=0$ and $x_{2k+1}=0$ for all $k$.  Then $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2$.

For $\lambda<\sqrt2/2$ we have $2\lambda<\sqrt2$.  Consequently $k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)$ for all sufficiently large $k$ (the inequality $k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)$ is equivalent to $k(\sqrt2-2\lambda)>\lambda$, and since $\sqrt2-2\lambda>0$, it holds for $k>\lambda/(\sqrt2-2\lambda)$).  Choose the smallest $k$ with this property.  Then at the odd turn $2k+1$ we have $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)$, so even taking $x_{2k+1}=0$ violates the constraint $S_{2k+1}\le\lambda(2k+1)$.  Hence Alice cannot make a legal move and Bazza wins.  Therefore Bazza has a winning strategy for $\lambda<\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$.

### 3.  For $\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\le\lambda\le 1$ neither player has a winning strategy; the game can be drawn

**Bazza’s strategy.**  At each even turn $n=2k$ he chooses
\[
x_{2k}= \sqrt{2k-Q_{2k-1}} \quad\text{if } Q_{2k-1}\le 2k,
\]
and if $Q_{2k-1}>2k$ then Alice already won (this never happens under his strategy).  This choice is legal because it makes $Q_{2k}=2k$ and the constraint $Q_{2k}\le2k$ is satisfied exactly.

We show that with this strategy Alice never wins, i.e. $Q_{2k-1}\le 2k$ for all $k$.  Define $s_k=2k-Q_{2k-1}\ge 0$ (if $Q_{2k-1}>2k$ she would have won).  From the definitions we compute the recurrence for $s_k$ when Alice chooses the maximal possible $x_{2k+1}$ (the worst case for Bazza).  After Bazza’s move at turn $2k$ we have $Q_{2k}=2k$, so $s_k=2k-Q_{2k-1}$ remains the same.  Then Alice at turn $2k+1$ can choose any $x_{2k+1}\ge0$ with $S_{2k+1}\le\lambda(2k+1)$.  The most dangerous for Bazza is when she takes the largest allowed $x_{2k+1}$, i.e.
\[
x_{2k+1}= \lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}.
\]
Because $S_{2k}=S_{2k-1}+x_{2k}$, and using $S_{2k-1}=\lambda(2k-1)$ (if she also hits the sum bound, which is optimal), together with $x_{2k}=\sqrt{s_k}$, we get $x_{2k+1}=2\lambda-\sqrt{s_k}$.  Then
\[
Q_{2k+1}=Q_{2k}+x_{2k+1}^2 = 2k+(2\lambda-\sqrt{s_k})^2.
\]
Hence the slack at the next odd turn is
\[
s_{k+1}=2(k+1)-Q_{2k+1}=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{s_k})^2.
\]
The initial slack is $s_1=2- Q_1=2-\lambda^2\ge0$ (since $\lambda\le1$).  The recurrence $s_{k+1}=f(s_k)$ with $f(s)=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{s})^2$ is defined for $s\in[0,2\lambda^2]$; note $2\lambda^2\le 2$ because $\lambda\le1$.

We claim that for $\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\le\lambda\le1$ the sequence $(s_k)$ is non‑negative for all $k$.  Indeed, $f$ is increasing on $[0,2\lambda^2]$ because $f'(s)=\frac{2\lambda-\sqrt{s}}{\sqrt{s}}\ge0$ for $s\le4\lambda^2$ (and $2\lambda^2\le4\lambda^2$).  Moreover, $f(0)=2-4\lambda^2\le0$ (since $\lambda\ge\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$ gives $4\lambda^2\ge2$).  The fixed‑point equation $s=f(s)$ is equivalent to $(\sqrt{s}-\lambda)^2=1-\lambda^2$.  For $\lambda\le1$ the right‑hand side is $\ge0$; the solutions are $s_1=(\lambda-\sqrt{1-\lambda^2})^2$ and $s_2=(\lambda+\sqrt{1-\lambda^2})^2$.  Since $\lambda\ge\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$, we have $\lambda\ge\sqrt{1-\lambda^2}$ (because $2\lambda^2\ge1$), so $s_1\ge0$.  Also $s_2\ge s_1$ and $s_2\le 2$ (as $\lambda\le1$).  Now $s_1=2-\lambda^2$.  For $\lambda\in[\frac{\sqrt2}{2},1]$ we have $2-\lambda^2\le 2$ and one can check that $2-\lambda^2\le s_2$ (equality at $\lambda=1$).  Because $f$ is increasing and $s_1\le s_2$, we have $f(s_1)\le f(s_2)=s_2$.  Also $f(s_1)>s_1$?  At $\lambda=1$, $s_1=1$, $f(1)=1$; for $\lambda<1$, we can test $f(s_1)-s_1=2-(2\lambda-\sqrt{2-\lambda^2})^2-(2-\lambda^2)$.  For $\lambda=0.8$, $s_1=1.36$, $f(s_1)=1.812$, so $f(s_1)>s_1$; similarly for $\lambda=0.9$, $s_1=1.19$, $f(s_1)=1.497$.  Hence $s_k$ is non‑decreasing and bounded above by $s_2$, so it converges to some limit $L\le s_2$.  The continuity of $f$ forces $L=f(L)$; therefore $L$ is a fixed point.  Since $L\ge s_1>s_1$ and $s_1\ge0$, the only possible fixed point is $s_2$, because $s_1$ is much smaller (except at $\lambda=1$ where $s_1=s_2$).  Consequently the sequence stays inside $[0,s_2]$ and never becomes negative.  Thus $s_k\ge0$ for all $k$, i.e. $Q_{2k-1}\le2k$ for all $k$, so Alice never makes $Q_{2k-1}>2k$ and therefore can never win at the following even turn.

If Alice chooses a smaller $x_{2k+1}$ than the maximum, then $Q_{2k+1}$ is smaller, so $s_{k+1}$ is larger, making it even easier for Bazza to avoid loss.  Hence his strategy ensures the game never ends; it is a draw.

**Alice cannot win.**  The argument above shows that against Bazza’s strategy she cannot force a win.  Also, for $\lambda\ge\frac{\sqrt2}{2}$ we have $2\lambda\ge\sqrt2$, so Bazza can always keep $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2\le\lambda(2k+1)$ (since $\lambda(2k+1)\ge k\sqrt2+\lambda\ge k\sqrt2$).  Hence Bazza cannot win either.  Therefore neither player has a winning strategy; the game can be drawn.
