\(f(x) \ge \sqrt2\) (by the AM‚ÄìGM inequality: \(x^2 + ( \sqrt{2-x^2})^2 = 2\), so by Cauchy‚ÄìSchwarz \((x+\sqrt{2-x^2})^2 \le 2\cdot2 =4\), but the minimum is attained at the endpoints? Actually the function \(f(x)\) attains its minimum \(\sqrt2\) at \(x=0\) and \(x=\sqrt2\), and its maximum \(2\) at \(x=1\); hence \(f(x)\ge\sqrt2\)).  Therefore  
\[
d_{k+1} \le d_k + 2\lambda - \sqrt2.
\]  
Because \(\lambda < \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\), we have \(2\lambda - \sqrt2 < 0\).  Thus \(d_{k+1} < d_k\) for every \(k\), regardless of Alice‚Äôs choice.  Since \(d_0 = \lambda > 0\), the sequence \(\{d_k\}\) is strictly decreasing and bounded below by \(0\).  Consequently there exists a smallest index \(k_0\) for which \(d_{k_0} < 0\).  At that moment \(s_{k_0} > \lambda(2k_0+1)\).  The next odd turn is \(n=2k_0+1\), and the condition requires \(S_n \le \lambda n\).  But the sum before her move is already \(s_{k_0} > \lambda(2k_0+1)\), so even choosing \(x_{2k_0+1}=0\) would violate the bound.  Hence Alice has no legal move and Bazza wins.  

Thus Bazza has a winning strategy for every \(\lambda < \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).

---

### 3.  The game is a draw when \(\lambda = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\)

We show that neither player can force a win.  

**Bazza cannot win:** If Alice always chooses \(x_{odd}=0\), then on each even turn Bazza plays the maximal move \(x_{2k}=\sqrt2\) (since \(Q_{2k-1}=2k-2\)).  This gives \(S_{2k}=k\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2k}=2k\).  Then at the start of an odd turn \(2k+1\) we have  
\(S_{2k}=k\sqrt2\) and the bound is \(\lambda(2k+1)=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}(2k+1)=k\sqrt2+\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  Hence \(S_{2k} < \lambda(2k+1)\) by \(\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  Alice can indeed choose \(x_{2k+1}=0\) (the slack is positive) and the game continues forever.  Therefore Bazza does not have a winning strategy.

**Alice cannot win:** For any odd turn \(n=2k+1\) we have the slack \(\sigma_k = \lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}\).  If Alice chooses \(0\) on all her turns, the sum after Bazza‚Äôs moves is \(S_{2k}=k\sqrt2\) as above, so \(\sigma_k = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}(2k+1)-k\sqrt2 = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  To win she would need to make \(Q_{2k+1} > 2k+2\), which requires \(x_{2k+1} > \sqrt2\), but the maximum she can choose is at most \(\sigma_k = \frac{\sqrt2}{2} < \sqrt2\).  Thus she cannot win with this play.  If she ever chooses a positive \(x\) on some earlier odd turn, the cumulative sum \(S\) increases, reducing future slacks, making it even harder to exceed \(\sqrt2\).  Hence no matter how Alice plays, she can never force \(Q_n > n+1\) at the end of an odd turn.  Therefore Alice has no winning strategy either.

Consequently the game can go on indefinitely if both players play optimally (Alice chooses \(0\) on odd turns, Bazza chooses the maximum on even turns), so neither player wins.

### 4.  Conclusion

- Alice has a winning strategy if and only if \(\lambda > \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  
- Bazza has a winning strategy if and only if \(\lambda < \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  
- If \(\lambda = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}\), the game can continue forever and neither player has a winning strategy.  

\[
\boxed{\text{Alice wins for }\lambda>\frac{\sqrt2}{2},\quad \text{Bazza wins for }\lambda<\frac{\sqrt2}{2},\quad \text{draw for }\lambda=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}}
\]
