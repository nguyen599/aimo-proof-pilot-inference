We consider the game with sequence \(x_1,x_2,\dots\) and cumulative sums  
\(S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\), \(Q_n=\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2\).  
At odd \(n\) Alice chooses \(x_n\ge0\) with \(S_n\le\lambda n\);  
at even \(n\) Bazza chooses \(x_n\ge0\) with \(Q_n\le n\).  
If a player cannot make a legal move the other wins; otherwise the game continues forever.

We determine all \(\lambda>0\) for which Alice (resp. Bazza) has a winning strategy.

**Lemma.** For any \(n\) and any real numbers \(x_1,\dots,x_n\) we have  
\((S_n)^2\le n\,Q_n\) (Cauchy–Schwarz).

### 1. Bazza wins when \(\lambda<\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\)

Bazza’s strategy: on each even turn \(n=2k\) he chooses the maximal admissible number, i.e.  
\(x_{2k}=\sqrt{2k-Q_{2k-1}}\) (the square‑root is defined because the constraint \(Q_{2k}\le2k\) is the same as \(Q_{2k-1}+x_{2k}^2\le2k\)).

We first argue that this strategy is as powerful as possible: no matter how Alice plays, the sum after Bazza’s move is at least the sum he would obtain if Alice always played \(0\).  
Suppose before Alice’s move at round \(k\) we have \(A=a\) and \(Q=q\).  
Alice chooses \(x\ge0\) with \(a+x\le\lambda(2k-1)\).  
After her move the state is \((a+x,\;q+x^2)\).  
Bazza then picks \(y=\sqrt{2k-(q+x^2)}\).  
The new sum is  
\[
A_{2k}=a+x+\sqrt{2k-q-x^2}.
\]  
For fixed \(c=2k-q\ge0\) the function \(f(x)=x+\sqrt{c-x^2}\) satisfies \(f(x)\ge\sqrt{c}\) for all \(x\in[0,\sqrt{c}]\) (the minimum occurs at the endpoints).  
Hence  
\[
A_{2k}\ge a+\sqrt{2k-q}=a+\sqrt{2k-q}.
\]  
The right‑hand side is exactly the sum that would occur if Alice had played \(x=0\).  
Thus the minimal possible \(A_{2k}\) is attained when Alice chooses \(x=0\).  
Consequently, if Bazza can force a win when Alice always plays \(0\), he wins against every strategy of Alice.

Now assume Alice plays \(x_n=0\) for all her turns.  
Then at turn \(2\) Bazza’s constraint is \(Q_2\le2\); because \(Q_1=0\) he can take \(x_2=\sqrt2\).  
By induction, before his move at turn \(2k\) we have \(Q_{2k-1}=2(k-1)\) and he chooses \(x_{2k}=\sqrt2\), so  
\[
A_{2k}=k\sqrt2,\qquad Q_{2k}=2k.
\]  
He wins if at some even turn the sum exceeds the bound for the next odd turn:  
\(A_{2k}>\lambda(2k+1)\).  
This inequality is equivalent to  
\[
k\sqrt2>\lambda(2k+1)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad k(\sqrt2-2\lambda)>\lambda.
\]  
Since \(\lambda<\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\) we have \(\sqrt2-2\lambda>0\).  
Choosing \(k>\dfrac{\lambda}{\sqrt2-2\lambda}\) makes the inequality true.  
At such a turn, after Bazza’s move we have \(A_{2k}>\lambda(2k+1)\); then Alice on her next turn must choose \(x_{2k+1}\ge0\) with \(A_{2k}+x_{2k+1}\le\lambda(2k+1)\), which is impossible because already \(A_{2k}>\lambda(2k+1)\).  
Thus Alice cannot move and Bazza wins.  

So **Bazza has a winning strategy for every \(\lambda<\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\)**.

### 2. Alice wins when \(\lambda>\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\)

Alice’s strategy: she plays \(x_n=0\) on every one of her turns until the first odd turn \(n=2k-1\) where the gap \(\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2}\) exceeds \(\sqrt2\).  
At that turn she chooses the maximal admissible number, i.e. \(x_{2k-1}=\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2}\).  
We show that such a turn exists and that after her move Bazza cannot make a legal move.

Because Alice plays \(0\) on all earlier turns, the state before her move at turn \(2k-1\) is  
\(A_{2k-2}=A_{2(k-1)}\) and \(Q_{2k-2}=Q_{2(k-1)}\).  
Bazza’s optimal play (to try to win) is to maximise \(A\), which forces him to choose \(y=\sqrt2\) on each of his turns; then  
\(A_{2(k-1)}=(k-1)\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2(k-1)}=2(k-1)\).  
For any other play of Bazza the value of \(A_{2k-2}\) is smaller, so the gap \(\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2}\) is larger than the value we obtain when he plays \(\sqrt2\).  
Hence the minimal possible gap is  
\[
\lambda(2k-1)-(k-1)\sqrt2.
\]  
Since \(\lambda>\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\), the coefficient \(2\lambda-\sqrt2\) is positive, and  
\[
\lambda(2k-1)-(k-1)\sqrt2 = (2\lambda-\sqrt2)k + \bigl(\lambda-\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\bigr)
\]  
grows linearly with \(k\).  
Therefore for sufficiently large \(k\) we have  
\[
\lambda(2k-1)-(k-1)\sqrt2 > \sqrt2.
\]  
At that \(k\) the actual gap is at least this value, so  
\[
\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2} > \sqrt2.
\]  
Now Alice plays \(x=\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2}\) (which is allowed because she chooses the maximum).  
After her move  
\[
Q_{2k-1}=Q_{2k-2}+x^2.
\]  
Since \(Q_{2k-2}\le 2k-2\) (by the previous even bound) and \(x^2>2\), we obtain  
\[
Q_{2k-1} > 2k-2+2 = 2k.
\]  
Thus at Bazza’s next turn (turn \(2k\)) he must choose \(y\ge0\) with \(Q_{2k-1}+y^2\le2k\), but already \(Q_{2k-1}>2k\) so even \(y=0\) violates the constraint.  
Hence Bazza cannot make a legal move and Alice wins.

Therefore **Alice has a winning strategy for every \(\lambda>\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\)**.

### 3. The case \(\lambda=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\)

We show that neither player can force a win; the game can continue indefinitely.

Consider the following play: Alice always chooses \(x_n=0\) on her turns.  
Bazza always chooses \(x_{2k}=\sqrt2\) (the maximum allowed) on his turns.  
Then after every even turn we have  
\[
A_{2k}=k\sqrt2,\qquad Q_{2k}=2k.
\]  
At an odd turn \(n=2k-1\) before Alice’s move we have  
\(A_{2k-2}=(k-1)\sqrt2\) and the bound for her move is \(\lambda(2k-1)=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}(2k-1)=\sqrt2k-\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  
The gap is  
\[
\lambda(2k-1)-A_{2k-2}= \bigl(\sqrt2k-\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\bigr)- (k-1)\sqrt2 = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}.
\]  
Since \(\frac{\sqrt2}{2}<\sqrt2\) she may safely play \(x=0\).  
Thus the game never ends, so no winning strategy exists for either player.

Could either player deviate to obtain a win?  
If Alice ever plays a positive number on her turn, say \(x>0\), then because the gap before her move is only \(\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\), we have \(x\le\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\).  
After her move the sum \(A\) increases by \(x\) and the sum of squares increases by \(x^2\).  
Then Bazza on his next turn can choose the maximal allowed \(y\); the function \(x+\sqrt{2-x^2}\) is increasing for \(x\in[0,\sqrt2]\), so the increase in his sum becomes larger than \(\sqrt2\).  
This makes it easier for Bazza to eventually exceed the bound, so Alice would lose.  
Hence playing positive does not help her.  

If Bazza ever plays a smaller \(y\) than \(\sqrt2\), then Alice’s gap becomes larger, making it easier for her to eventually win; but because she can always play \(0\) and Bazza’s optimal is to maximise \(A\), he will play \(\sqrt2\) to keep the gap minimal.  
Thus Bazza cannot force a win, and Alice cannot force a win either.  

Consequently, for \(\lambda=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\) the game is a draw (no player has a winning strategy).
