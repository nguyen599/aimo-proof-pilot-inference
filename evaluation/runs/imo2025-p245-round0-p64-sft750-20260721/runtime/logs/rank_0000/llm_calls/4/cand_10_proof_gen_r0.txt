We consider the infinite sequence \(a_1,a_2,\dots\) of positive integers such that each \(a_n\) has at least three proper divisors and for every \(n\ge 1\),

\[
a_{n+1}=d_1+d_2+d_3,
\]

where \(d_1>d_2>d_3\) are the three largest proper divisors of \(a_n\).

We determine all possible values of \(a_1\) for which such a sequence can be defined indefinitely (i.e. every term has at least three proper divisors).

---
### 1.  Basic observations
For a positive integer \(N>1\) let \(f(N)\) denote the sum of its three largest proper divisors.  
The condition “has at least three proper divisors” is equivalent to \(\tau(N)\ge 4\) (where \(\tau\) is the number of positive divisors), because the proper divisors are all divisors except \(N\) itself.  
Thus every term of the sequence lies in the set

\[
S=\{N>1:\tau(N)\ge 4\}.
\]

### 2.  No term can be odd
Assume that some term \(a_n\) is odd.  
Let \(p\) be its smallest prime factor; then \(p\ge 3\) and \(p\) is odd.  
The three largest proper divisors of \(a_n\) are \(a_n/p\), \(a_n/d_2\), \(a_n/d_3\) where \(d_2,d_3\) are the second and third smallest divisors \(>1\) (all odd because \(a_n\) is odd).  
Hence each of these three numbers is odd, so \(a_{n+1}\) is odd.  
Moreover, \(1/p+1/d_2+1/d_3<1\) because \(p\ge 3\) and \(d_2,d_3\ge p\) with strict inequality for at least one of them (since \(d_2>p\)).  
Thus \(a_{n+1}=a_n\cdot(1/p+1/d_2+1/d_3)<a_n\).  
The sequence of odd terms would be strictly decreasing and bounded below by \(1\); therefore it must eventually reach a number with fewer than three proper divisors (a prime or a prime square).
