* Next, we show \(a\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\).  Let \(k=\frac{e-1}{2}\).  After \(k\) steps we reach a term \(N=a_k\) with \(v_2(N)=1\).  Write \(N=2\cdot3^{a-k}\cdot m\).  Since the sequence is infinite, \(N\) must have at least three proper divisors.  For \(N\) to be a fixed point (so that the sequence becomes constant and thus infinite) we need the third smallest divisor of \(N\) to be \(6\).  This requires that \(N\) is not divisible by \(4\) (true because \(v_2=1\)) and not divisible by \(5\) (true because \(5\nmid m\)).  Moreover, we need that the divisor \(3\) appears (i.e. \(a-k\ge1\)), otherwise the three smallest divisors would be \(2\), an odd prime \(p\ge5\) (if \(m>1\) and \(p\mid m\)) and \(p'\) etc., and then \(f(N)<N\) would cause a decrease.  Thus we must have \(a-k\ge1\), i.e. \(a\ge k+1=\frac{e+1}{2}\).  If \(a<\frac{e+1}{2}\), then \(N\) is not divisible by \(3\) and the sequence would become strictly decreasing from that point, contradiction.  Therefore the condition holds.

Thus any possible \(a_1\) must satisfy: \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\), \(5\nmid a_1\), and if we write \(a_1=2^e\cdot3^a\cdot t\) with \(t\) odd and \(\gcd(t,15)=1\), then \(e\) is odd and \(a\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\).

**Sufficiency.**  Let \(a_1\) be a positive integer with the properties above.  Then:

* If \(e=1\), then \(a_1=2\cdot3^a\cdot t\) with \(a\ge1\) and \(\gcd(t,15)=1\).  Since \(a_1\) is not divisible by \(4\) or \(5\), the three smallest divisors \(>1\) are \(2,3,6\); hence \(f(a_1)=a_1\).  The sequence is constant, and every term is composite with at least the divisors \(1,2,3,6\), so the sequence is infinite.
* If \(e\ge3\) (odd), then \(a_2=13a_1/12=13\cdot2^{e-2}\cdot3^{a-1}\cdot t\).  Now \(e-2\) is odd and \(a-1\ge\frac{e+1}{2}-1=\frac{e-1}{2}=\frac{(e-2)+1}{2}\); thus the conditions are preserved.  After \(k=\frac{e-1}{2}\) steps we reach
\[
N=a_k=2\cdot3^{a-k}\cdot t.
\]
Because \(a\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\), we have \(a-k\ge1\).  Hence \(N\) is a multiple of \(6\) and not divisible by \(5\).  Moreover, \(v_2(N)=1\), so the three smallest divisors of \(N\) are \(2,3,6\) and therefore \(f(N)=N\).  Thus the sequence becomes constant from that point onward, and all terms have at least three proper divisors.  Hence the sequence is infinite.

Therefore the set of all possible values of \(a_1\) is exactly the set of positive integers that are multiples of \(6\), not divisible by \(5\), and such that if \(e=v_2(a_1)\) and \(a=v_3(a_1)\), then \(e\) is odd and \(a\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\).

Equivalently, \(a_1=2^{2k+1}\cdot3^{k+1}\cdot u\) where \(k\ge0\) and \(u\) is any odd integer with \(\gcd(u,15)=1\), together with all numbers having larger exponent of \(3\) (i.e. any \(m\ge k+1\) with the same \(e\) and \(t\) coprime to \(15\)).  In closed form:
\[
\boxed{\{a_1\in\mathbb{N} : 6\mid a_1,\ 5\nmid a_1,\ v_2(a_1)\text{ odd},\ v_3(a_1)\ge\frac{v_2(a_1)+1}{2}\}.
\]
