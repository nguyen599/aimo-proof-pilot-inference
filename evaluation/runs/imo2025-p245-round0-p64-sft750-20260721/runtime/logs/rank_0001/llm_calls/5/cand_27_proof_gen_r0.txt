The fixed‑point equation \(x=2\lambda-\sqrt{2-x^2}\) has two non‑negative roots:  
\(x_-=\lambda-\sqrt{1-\lambda^2}\) and \(x_*=\lambda+\sqrt{1-\lambda^2}\).  
Because \(\lambda<1\), \(x_*<\sqrt2\). Moreover \(x_-\) is the smaller root.  
If Alice always chooses the maximum allowed, the iterates \(a_{k+1}=g(a_k)\) with \(a_1\le\lambda\) converge to \(x_-\); in particular all \(a_k\) are less than \(\sqrt2\) and hence \(a_k^2<2\).  
Thus \(Q_{2k-1}=2(k-1)+a_k^2<2k\), so Bazza’s bound is never exceeded at any even turn.  
If she chooses a smaller number, \(Q\) becomes even smaller, so she cannot make Bazza’s sum of squares exceed the bound.  
Therefore Bazza’s strategy guarantees that the game continues forever; Alice cannot win.  
Hence for \(\frac1{\sqrt2}\le\lambda<1\) neither player has a winning strategy (the game can be drawn).

**Conclusion:**  
- Alice has a winning strategy iff \(\lambda\ge1\).  
- Bazza has a winning strategy iff \(0<\lambda<\frac1{\sqrt2}\).  
- For \(\frac1{\sqrt2}\le\lambda<1\) the game is a draw.

\boxed{\text{Alice wins for }\lambda\ge1,\quad \text{Bazza wins for }0<\lambda<\frac1{\sqrt2},\quad \text{draw for }\frac1{\sqrt2}\le\lambda<1.}
