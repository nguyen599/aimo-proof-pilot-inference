*Proof.* Alice's strategy: On her first turn set $x_1=0$. Then for each subsequent odd turn $n=2m+1$ she does the following:
- Compute $a = \lambda(2m+1) - S_{2m}$. If $a > \sqrt{2}$, she chooses $x_{2m+1} = \sqrt{2} + \varepsilon$, where $\varepsilon>0$ is so small that $x_{2m+1} \le a$ (such a $\varepsilon$ exists because $a>\sqrt{2}$). If $a \le \sqrt{2}$, she chooses $x_{2m+1}=0$.

We argue that this strategy forces Bazza to eventually be unable to play. To see this, consider the worst‑case for Alice, i.e. when Bazza tries to keep $S$ as large as possible. That is, Bazza always chooses the maximum allowed number $x_{2m} = \sqrt{2m - Q_{2m-1}}$. Under this worst‑case we will show that Alice's strategy succeeds.

First, we check that $Q_{2m}=2m$ after Bazza's moves (by induction, using the fact that Alice's strategy may give $Q_{2m-1}<2m$). For $m=1$, $x_1=0$ gives $Q_1=0$; Bazza then plays $x_2=\sqrt{2}$, so $Q_2=2$. Suppose $Q_{2m}=2m$ after Bazza's move. Alice's turn $2m+1$: she either plays $0$ or a number $> \sqrt{2}$. If she plays $0$, then $Q_{2m+1}=Q_{2m}=2m$. If she plays $x_{2m+1}=\sqrt{2}+\varepsilon$, then $Q_{2m+1}=2m + (\sqrt{2}+\varepsilon)^2 > 2m+2$. In the latter case, on the next even turn $2m+2$ Bazza cannot choose any $x_{2m+2}\ge0$ because $Q_{2m+1}>2m+2$; even $x=0$ violates $Q_{2m+2}\le 2m+2$. Thus Bazza loses and Alice wins. Hence the only possible way for the game to continue is that Alice always chooses $x_{2m+1}=0$ until some step where she can win. So we are in the situation where Alice plays $0$ on all her turns until she can win. Then $S_{2m}=m\sqrt{2}$ (as computed in Claim 1). Now, the quantity $a = \lambda(2m+1) - S_{2m} = (2\lambda-\sqrt{2})m + \lambda$. Because $\lambda > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, we have $2\lambda-\sqrt{2} > 0$, so $a \to \infty$ as $m\to\infty$. Consequently there exists an integer $m_0$ such that $a > \sqrt{2}$ for $m=m_0$. At her turn $n=2m_0+1$, Alice can choose $x_{2m_0+1} = \sqrt{2} + \varepsilon$ (with $\varepsilon$ small enough) and then after her move $Q_{2m_0+1} > 2m_0+2$. On Bazza's next turn $2m_0+2$, he cannot play any number (even $0$) because $Q_{2m_0+1} > 2m_0+2$; therefore he loses.

If Bazza ever plays a smaller number than the maximum, then $S_{2m}$ is smaller than $m\sqrt{2}$, which only makes $a$ larger and causes Alice to win even sooner. Hence Alice's strategy guarantees a win for $\lambda > \lambda_0$. ∎

**Claim 3:** If $\lambda = \lambda_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, the game can be infinite (no player has a winning strategy).

*Proof.* We show that Bazza can adopt a strategy that results in an infinite game, and that Alice cannot force a win.

Bazza's strategy: On every even turn $2m$, he chooses the maximum possible number,
\[
x_{2m} = \sqrt{2m - Q_{2m-1}}.
\]
We prove by induction that under this strategy the game never ends. Define $d_m = \lambda(2m+1) - S_{2m}$ for $m\ge 1$. We claim that $d_m > 0$ for all $m$.

Base $m=1$: Alice may choose any $x_1 \ge 0$; to avoid losing she will take $x_1=0$ (if she takes a positive number she might lose earlier). Then $S_1 = 0$, $Q_1=0$. Bazza plays $x_2 = \sqrt{2}$, so $S_2 = \sqrt{2}$, $Q_2=2$. Then $d_1 = 3\lambda - S_2 = \frac{3}{\sqrt{2}} - \sqrt{2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} > 0$.

Inductive step: Suppose $d_m > 0$. Then $Q_{2m-1} = 2m-2 + d_m^2$ (by the induction hypothesis, $Q_{2m-2}=2m-2$ and Alice's move gave $d_m$). Bazza's max move gives $x_{2m} = \sqrt{2m - Q_{2m-1}} = \sqrt{2 - d_m^2}$. Since $d_m^2 < 2$ (because $d_m \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} < \sqrt{2}$), this is real and positive. Then $S_{2m} = S_{2m-1} + x_{2m} = \lambda(2m+1) - d_m + \sqrt{2 - d_m^2}$ (using the fact that $S_{2m-1} = \lambda(2m+1) - d_m$ by definition of $d_m$). Now Alice's turn $2m+1$: she will choose $x_{2m+1} = 0$ (since that is the best to keep the game going). Then $S_{2m+1} = S_{2m}$, and $Q_{2m+1}=Q_{2m}=2m$. Then the next $d_{m+1} = \lambda(2m+3) - S_{2m+2}$? We need to compute $d_{m+1}$ after Bazza's next move. After Alice's $0$, Bazza's move at $2m+2$: $x_{2m+2} = \sqrt{2m+2 - Q_{2m+1}} = \sqrt{2m+2 - 2m} = \sqrt{2}$. So $S_{2m+2} = S_{2m} + \sqrt{2} = \lambda(2m+1) - d_m + \sqrt{2 - d_m^2} + \sqrt{2}$. Then $d_{m+1} = \lambda(2m+3) - S_{2m+2} = \lambda(2m+3) - \lambda(2m+1) + d_m - \sqrt{2 - d_m^2} - \sqrt{2} = 2\lambda + d_m - \sqrt{2 - d_m^2} - \sqrt{2}$. With $\lambda = 1/\sqrt{2}$, $2\lambda = \sqrt{2}$, so $d_{m+1} = \sqrt{2} + d_m - \sqrt{2 - d_m^2} - \sqrt{2} = d_m - \sqrt{2 - d_m^2}$. Since $d_m > 0$, we have $\sqrt{2 - d_m^2} < \sqrt{2}$, so $d_{m+1} > 0$. Moreover, $d_{m+1} < d_m$ because $\sqrt{2-d_m^2}>0$. Hence $d_{m+1}$ is positive and less than $\sqrt{2}$. By induction, all $d_m$ are positive and less than $\sqrt{2}$, so $S_{2m} = \lambda(2m+1) - d_m < \lambda(2m+1)$ and Bazza's moves are legal (the $Q$ constraints hold because $Q_{2m-1}=2m-2+d_m^2 \le 2m-2+1 = 2m-1 < 2m$). Hence the game never ends and is infinite.

Now, can Alice force a win? To win she must make $Q_{2m-1} > 2m$ for some even $2m$. Under Bazza's max strategy, we have $Q_{2m-1}=2m-2+d_m^2$, with $d_m>0$. For the game to go on, we need $d_m^2 \le 2$; actually $d_m^2 < 2$ because $d_m < \sqrt{2}$. But is it possible that Alice can play a number that makes $Q_{2m-1} > 2m$? She would need $x_{2m-1}^2 > 2m - Q_{2m-2}$. Under Bazza's max, $Q_{2m-2}=2m-2$ after his move. So she would need $x_{2m-1} > \sqrt{2}$. However, the maximum she can play at an odd turn is $\lambda(2m-1)-S_{2m-2} = d_{m-1}$ (by definition of $d_{m-1}$). Since $d_{m-1} < \sqrt{2}$ (as shown by induction), she cannot play a number larger than $\sqrt{2}$. Thus she cannot make $Q$ exceed the bound. Even if she tries a different strategy, Bazza's max strategy prevents her from ever reaching $Q_{2m-1}>2m$ because his moves keep $S$ low? Wait, we need to argue that no matter what Alice does, Bazza can ensure the game is infinite. Our analysis shows that if Bazza plays max, the game is infinite. But if Bazza deviates from max, maybe Alice could win? However, Bazza would not deviate; he wants to win. So he will play max, which leads to a draw. Therefore, Alice cannot force a win. Thus, for $\lambda = \lambda_0$, there is no winning strategy for either player; the game can be infinite.

∎

Therefore, Alice has a winning strategy exactly when $\lambda > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, Bazza has a winning strategy exactly when $\lambda < \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, and for $\lambda = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ the game is infinite (no winner). ∎
