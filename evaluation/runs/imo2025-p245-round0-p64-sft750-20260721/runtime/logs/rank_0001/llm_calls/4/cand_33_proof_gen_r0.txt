  For odd numbers we have \(f(N)<N\) (Lemma 1), so the sequence from \(a_{t+1}\) strictly decreases.  
  Since it cannot be infinite, it must eventually reach a number with at most two proper divisors, contradicting the condition.  
  Hence \(e\) cannot be even; therefore \(e\) is odd.

* **Relation between \(e\) and \(k\)**:  
  Write \(e=2t+1\) with \(t\ge0\). Then after \(t\) steps  
  \[
  a_{t+1}=2^{1}\cdot 3^{k-t}\cdot 13^t L.
  \]  
  For the sequence to be infinite, the process cannot continue to decrease without bound.  
  If \(k-t=0\) then \(a_{t+1}=2\cdot13^t L\) is even and not divisible by \(3\).  
  As shown in §2, such numbers satisfy \(f(N)<N\) and the whole orbit would be strictly decreasing, impossible.  
  Thus we must have \(k-t\ge1\), i.e. \(k\ge t+1\).  
  Since \(e=2t+1\), this is \(k\ge \frac{e+1}{2}\).

* **No factor \(5\) in \(L\)**:  
  If \(5\mid L\) then \(a_{t+1}\) is divisible by \(5\).  
  For such a number, because \(e=1\) and \(3\mid a_{t+1}\), the three largest proper divisors become \(a/2,\; a/3,\; a/5\) (since \(a/5>a/6\)).  
  Then \(f(a_{t+1})=a_{t+1}\cdot\frac{31}{30}>a_{t+1}\) and the result is odd.  
  The odd number obtained then strictly decreases, eventually forcing a term with \(\tau(N)\le3\), a contradiction.  
  Hence \(5\nmid L\).

Therefore, if \(a_1\) works then  
\[
e\text{ is odd},\quad k\ge \frac{e+1}{2},\quad 5\nmid L.
\]

---

### 6.  Sufficiency  

Assume \(a_1=2^{2t+1}\cdot3^k\cdot L\) with \(t\ge0\), \(k\ge t+1\) and \(L\) odd, \(3\nmid L\), \(5\nmid L\).  
We verify that the sequence is infinite and every term has at least three proper divisors.

For \(i=0,1,\dots,t\) define  
\[
a_{i+1}= \frac{13}{12}a_i.
\]  
At each step we have \(a_i\) even and divisible by \(3\) (the exponent of \(2\) is \(\ge2\) until the last step), so the formula applies.  
After \(t\) steps  
\[
a_{t+1}=2\cdot3^{k-t}\cdot13^t L.
\]  
Because \(k\ge t+1\), we have \(k-t\ge1\); thus \(a_{t+1}\) is even, divisible by \(3\) and its odd part \(3^{k-t}13^t L\) is not divisible by \(5\) (since \(3,13,5\) are pairwise coprime and \(5\nmid L\)).  
For such a number the three largest proper divisors are \(a_{t+1}/2,\; a_{t+1}/3,\; a_{t+1}/6\) and their sum equals \(a_{t+1}\); hence \(a_{t+2}=a_{t+1}\) and the sequence becomes constant.

All terms before the constant are of the form  
\[
2^{2t+1-2i}\cdot3^{k-i}\cdot13^i L\quad (i=0,\dots,t-1).
\]  
For \(i\le t-1\) we have \(2t+1-2i\ge3\) and \(k-i\ge1\) (since \(k\ge t+1\) and \(i\le t-1\)), so each such number is even and divisible by \(3\); thus its divisor set contains \(1,2,3,6\) and consequently has at least four divisors, i.e. lies in \(S\).  
Therefore the whole sequence consists of numbers with at least three proper divisors, and it is infinite.  

Thus any number of the described form is admissible.

---

### 7.  Conclusion  

All possible values of \(a_1\) are exactly the positive integers that can be expressed as  
\[
a_1 = 2^{2t+1}\cdot3^k\cdot L,
\]  
where \(t\ge0\), \(k\ge t+1\), and \(L\) is an odd integer not divisible by \(3\) or \(5\).  
Equivalently, when writing \(a_1 = 2^e\cdot3^k\cdot m\) with \(m\) odd and \(3\nmid m\), we must have  
\(e\) odd, \(k\ge \frac{e+1}{2}\), and \(5\nmid m\).

\[
\boxed{a_1 = 2^{2t+1}3^k L,\quad t\ge0,\;k\ge t+1,\;L\text{ odd},\;3\nmid L,\;5\nmid L}
\]  
(where the expression is understood as a representation of the integer.)
