Because the sequence decreases and is bounded below, it must eventually become a number \(N\) with \(\tau(N)\le 3\); otherwise it would decrease indefinitely without ever becoming a prime or a prime square, which is impossible. But a number with \(\tau(N)\le 3\) has fewer than three proper divisors, contradicting the requirement that every term has at least three proper divisors. Hence such an \(a_1\) is impossible. ∎

Therefore any valid \(a_1\) must be a multiple of \(6\). Write \(a_1=6k\) with \(k\in\mathbb{N}\).

**Analysis of multiples of \(6\).**  
Let \(N=6k\). The three largest proper divisors of \(N\) are:

- \(d_1=N/2=3k\),
- \(d_2=N/3=2k\) (since \(3\mid N\)),
- \(d_3\) is the largest divisor among the remaining proper divisors.  
  If \(k\) is odd, then \(N\) is not divisible by \(4\); the next candidate is \(N/6=k\) if \(5\nmid k\) (otherwise \(N/5\) could be larger than \(k\) but if \(5\mid k\) then \(N\) is divisible by \(5\) and \(N/5\) is a divisor; we will treat this case separately).  
  If \(k\) is even, then \(N\) is divisible by \(12\) and we have \(d_3=N/4\) (since \(N/4>N/6\)).

We consider two possibilities.

**Case 1: \(k\) is odd.**  
Then \(N\) is not divisible by \(4\). The divisors of \(N\) are \(1,2,3,6\) and possibly others from \(k\). The three largest proper divisors are exactly \(3k,2k,k\) if \(5\nmid k\); if \(5\mid k\) then \(N/5\) is an integer and is larger than \(k\), so the three largest become \(3k,2k,N/5\) and their sum is \(5k+N/5=5k+6k/5=31k/5\).  
- If \(5\nmid k\), then \(f(N)=3k+2k+k=6k=N\), so \(N\) is a fixed point. It has \(\tau(N)\ge4\) (since it is a multiple of \(6\) and not of \(4\)), so the sequence is constant and valid.  
- If \(5\mid k\), then \(k=5t\) with \(t\) odd. Then \(f(N)=31t\), which is odd and not a multiple of \(6\). By Lemma 1, the subsequent terms will strictly decrease and eventually hit a number with fewer than three proper divisors, contradicting the condition. Hence such \(a_1\) are invalid.

**Case 2: \(k\) is even.**  
Then \(N\) is divisible by \(12\). The three largest proper divisors are \(3k,2k\) and \(k/2\)? Wait: \(N=12m\) with \(m=k/2\). Actually \(k=2m\), so \(N=12m\). Then the largest proper divisor is \(6m\), the second is \(4m\), and the third is \(3m\) (since \(N/4=3m> N/6=2m\)). Hence \(f(N)=6m+4m+3m=13m=13k/2\).

The same reasoning applies to \(k_2\): if \(k_2\) is odd and not divisible by \(5\), then \(a_2\) is a fixed point and the process stops; if \(k_2\) is odd and divisible by \(5\), the next step fails; if \(k_2\) is even, we again need it to be a multiple of \(12\) to continue.  

Iterating this, we define the sequence \(k_0=k\), and for \(i\ge0\):
\[
k_{i+1}=
\begin{cases}
13k_i/2 & \text{if } k_i \text{ is even},\\
k_i & \text{if } k_i \text{ is odd and }5\nmid k_i,\\
31k_i/5 & \text{if } k_i \text{ is odd and }5\mid k_i.
\end{cases}
\]
However, note that the transformation \(k_i\to 13k_i/2\) only yields an integer when \(k_i\) is even; we need the result to be a multiple of \(6\) to continue, which forces \(k_i\) to be a multiple of \(12\). Hence we can only apply the reduction \(k_i\to 13k_i/12\) when \(12\mid k_i\).  

Let \(r\) be the largest integer such that \(12^r\mid k\). Write \(k=12^r u\) with \(u\) not divisible by \(12\). Applying the transformation \(k\mapsto 13k/12\) \(r\) times gives \(k_r=13^r u\). Since \(u\) is not divisible by \(12\), \(k_r\) is not divisible by \(12\) (if \(u\) were even, it would have \(v_2(u)\ge1\) but then \(v_3(u)=0\) because otherwise \(12\) would divide \(u\); in that case \(k_r\) is even but not divisible by \(3\), so not divisible by \(12\)). For the sequence to be valid we need that when we reach a term that is not a multiple of \(12\), it must be a fixed point. That is, \(k_r=13^r u\) must be odd and not divisible by \(5\). Since \(13^r\) is odd, this requires \(u\) to be odd and \(5\nmid u\). Consequently, the necessary and sufficient condition for a valid \(a_1=6k\) is that \(k\) is of the form \(k=12^r u\) with \(r\ge0\) and \(u\) odd, \(5\nmid u\).  

Conversely, if \(k=12^r u\) with \(u\) odd and \(5\nmid u\), then the process described above yields after \(r\) steps the number \(a_{r+1}=6\cdot13^r u\). This number is a fixed point because it is a multiple of \(6\) and not divisible by \(4\) (the exponent of \(2\) is \(1\)) nor by \(5\) (since \(5\nmid u\)). All intermediate terms are multiples of \(12\) and therefore have at least four divisors. Thus the sequence is well‑defined and infinite.  

Therefore the possible values of \(a_1\) are exactly the positive integers of the form  
\[
a_1 = 6 \cdot 12^r \cdot u,

where \(r\) is a non‑negative integer and \(u\) is an odd positive integer not divisible by \(5\). Equivalently, \(a_1 = 2^{2r+1} \cdot 3^{r+1} \cdot u\) with the same conditions on \(r\) and \(u\).
