Actually \(p+q+1< pq\) for \(p,q\ge5\) because \(pq-p-q-1=(p-1)(q-1)-2\ge 4\cdot4-2=14>0\). If \(N\) is a prime power \(p^k\) with \(k\ge3\), then \(f(N)=p^{k-1}+p^{k-2}+p^{k-3}=p^{k-3}(p^2+p+1)\). Since \(p^2+p+1<p^3\) for \(p\ge2\), we get \(f(N)<N\). In both situations \(f(N)\) is odd (sum of three odd numbers), so it is not divisible by \(2\) and hence not by \(6\).  
* If \(p=2\) and \(3\nmid N\), then \(N\) is even but not a multiple of \(3\). The three smallest divisors are either \(2,4,r\) with \(r\ge5\) (if \(4\mid N\)) or \(2,q,2q\) with \(q\ge5\) (if \(4\nmid N\)). In the first subcase, \(f(N)=N/2+N/4+N/r=3N/4+N/r\). Since \(r\ge5\), we have \(f(N)\le 3N/4+N/5=19N/20<N\). Also, because \(N\) is not divisible by \(3\), we have \(3N/4\equiv0\pmod3\) (as \(N\) is divisible by \(4\)), so \(f(N)\equiv N/r\pmod3\). But \(N/r\) is an integer and \(3\nmid N\); hence \(3\nmid N/r\). Thus \(f(N)\not\equiv0\pmod3\), so \(f(N)\) is not a multiple of \(3\). Consequently \(f(N)\) is not a multiple of \(6\).  
In the second subcase, \(N=2q\) with \(q\) odd and \(3\nmid q\) (since \(3\nmid N\)), then \(f(N)=N/2+ N/q+1 = q+2+1 = q+3\). Because \(q\) is not divisible by \(3\), \(f(N)\equiv q\pmod3\neq0\); also \(f(N)\) is odd (since \(q\) is odd). Hence again \(f(N)\) is not a multiple of \(6\).

Therefore, for any \(N\) that is not a multiple of \(6\), we have \(f(N)<N\) and \(f(N)\) is also not a multiple of \(6\).  

Now we characterize all possible first terms \(a_1\) for which the sequence \(\{a_n\}\) is infinite and each term has at least three proper divisors.  

If \(a_1\) belongs to the set \(B=\{6\cdot12^j\cdot w\mid j\ge0,\ w\text{ odd},\ 5\nmid w\}\), then we verify that the sequence is infinite.  
Indeed, \(a_1\) is divisible by \(12\) if \(j\ge1\) and by \(6\) if \(j=0\); write \(a_1=6\cdot12^j\cdot w\). Then for \(j\ge1\) we have \(a_1=12\cdot(6\cdot12^{j-1}\cdot w)\), so \(f(a_1)=13\cdot(6\cdot12^{j-1}\cdot w) = 6\cdot12^{j-1}\cdot13w\). After \(j\) steps we obtain
\[
f^j(a_1) = 6\cdot(13^j w).
\]
Because \(w\) is odd and not divisible by \(5\), \(13^j w\) is also odd and not divisible by \(5\). Hence \(6\cdot(13^j w)\) is a fixed point (it is divisible by \(6\) but not by \(4\) and not by \(5\)). Therefore the sequence becomes constant from that point onward, and each term has at least three proper divisors. Thus the sequence is infinite.

Conversely, suppose the sequence \(\{a_n\}\) is infinite and each \(a_n\) has at least three proper divisors. Then the recurrence is well‑defined for all \(n\). We show that \(a_1\) must belong to \(B\).

First, if \(a_1\) were not a multiple of \(6\), then by the earlier observation, \(f(a_1)<a_1\) and \(f(a_1)\) is also not a multiple of \(6\). Since the sequence is infinite, every term must have at least three proper divisors; in particular \(f(a_1)\in S\) (the set of numbers with at least three proper divisors). Applying the same reasoning to \(f(a_1)\) we obtain an infinite strictly decreasing sequence of positive integers, which is impossible (the positive integers are well‑ordered). Therefore \(a_1\) must be a multiple of \(6\).

Now, if \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\) but not of the form \(6\cdot12^j\cdot w\) with \(w\) odd and \(5\nmid w\), then either \(a_1/6\) is odd and divisible by \(5\), or \(a_1/6\) is even but not a multiple of \(12\). In the first case, \(a_1\) is divisible by \(30\) and not by \(4\). Its three smallest divisors are \(2,3,5\), so
\[
f(a_1) = \frac{a_1}{2}+\frac{a_1}{3}+\frac{a_1}{5} = \frac{31a_1}{30}.
\]
Since \(a_1=30k\), we have \(f(a_1)=31k\). Because \(k\) is odd (otherwise \(a_1/6\) would be even), \(f(a_1)\) is odd and not divisible by \(3\) (as \(k\) is not a multiple of \(3\)), hence not a multiple of \(6\).  
In the second case, \(a_1=12t\) with \(t\) not of the form \(12^{j-1}\cdot w\) with \(w\) odd and \(5\nmid w\). Then \(f(a_1)=13t\). If \(t\) were not divisible by \(6\), then \(13t\) would not be a multiple of \(6\) (since \(\gcd(13,6)=1\) and \(t\) is not a multiple of \(6\)), so again \(f(a_1)\) is not a multiple of \(6\). If \(t\) were divisible by \(6\), then \(a_1=12t=72\cdot(t/6)\) would belong to \(B\), contradicting our assumption. Hence in all subcases \(f(a_1)\) is not a multiple of \(6\). Now, from the observation above, for a number that is not a multiple of \(6\) we have \(f(n)<n\) and \(f(n)\) remains not a multiple of \(6\). Consequently the sequence from \(f(a_1)\) is strictly decreasing and consists of numbers that are not multiples of \(6\). Since the sequence is infinite, it must eventually become a term that has fewer than three proper divisors, which is impossible. Thus such an \(a_1\) cannot lead to an infinite sequence.  

Therefore \(a_1\) must belong to \(B\).  

We conclude that the infinite sequence \(\{a_n\}\) exists if and only if \(a_1\) can be written as
\[
a_1 = 6\cdot12^j\cdot w,
\]
where \(j\ge0\) and \(w\) is an odd positive integer not divisible by \(5\).
