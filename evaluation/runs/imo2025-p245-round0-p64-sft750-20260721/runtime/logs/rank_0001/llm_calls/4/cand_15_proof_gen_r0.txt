The three smallest divisors greater than \(1\) are at least \(3,5,7\) (or possibly \(3,5,9\) etc.) and in any case the sum of their reciprocals is less than \(1\). Hence \(f(N)=N\cdot S< N\). Moreover, every proper divisor of an odd number is odd, so \(f(N)\) is odd. Thus the sequence starting from an odd \(a_1\) is strictly decreasing and consists of odd numbers. Since the sequence of positive integers cannot decrease forever, it must eventually reach a number with at most two proper divisors (a prime or the square of a prime). Such a number does not have three proper divisors, so the process cannot continue indefinitely. Therefore \(a_1\) cannot be odd.

Now suppose \(a_1\) is even but not divisible by \(3\). Then \(a_1=2k\) with \(k\) odd. The smallest prime factor is \(2\). The three smallest divisors greater than \(1\) are \(2\) and two other numbers each at least \(4\) (if \(4\mid a_1\)) or \(5\) (if \(5\mid a_1\) and \(4\nmid a_1\)) etc. In any case the sum of reciprocals is at most \(\frac12+\frac14+\frac15=\frac{19}{20}<1\), so \(f(a_1)<a_1\). Moreover, \(f(a_1)\) is either even or odd. If it is even and not divisible by \(3\), the same reasoning applies; if it becomes odd, we already know odd numbers fail. Thus the sequence is strictly decreasing and eventually hits a number with at most two proper divisors, contradicting the requirement that every term have at least three proper divisors. Hence \(a_1\) must be divisible by \(3\) as well, i.e. \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\).

\noindent\textbf{2. Analysis for numbers divisible by \(6\).}
Let \(a_1=6k\). The positive divisors of \(6k\) include \(1,2,3\). The third smallest divisor greater than \(1\) is
\[
d = \begin{cases}
4 & \text{if }4\mid 6k,\\
5 & \text{if }4\nmid 6k\text{ and }5\mid 6k,\\
6 & \text{if }4\nmid 6k\text{ and }5\nmid 6k.
\end{cases}
\]
We consider the three possibilities.

\noindent\textit{Case A: } \(4\nmid 6k\) and \(5\nmid 6k\). Then the three largest proper divisors are \(\frac{6k}{2}=3k,\frac{6k}{3}=2k,\frac{6k}{6}=k\). Their sum is \(3k+2k+k=6k = a_1\). Hence \(f(a_1)=a_1\). The number is a fixed point. Because it is a multiple of \(6\), it has at least three proper divisors (e.g. \(1,2,3\) are proper). Thus the sequence is constant and valid. Here \(k\) is odd (since \(4\nmid 6k\) implies the exponent of \(2\) is exactly \(1\), so \(k\) odd) and also \(5\nmid k\) (because \(5\nmid 6k\) gives \(5\nmid k\)). So \(a_1=6k\) with \(k\) odd and \(5\nmid k\) are admissible.

\noindent\textit{Case B: } \(4\mid 6k\) and \(5\nmid 6k\). Write \(6k=12m\). Then \(a_1=12m\) and \(m\) is an integer (not necessarily odd). The three smallest divisors greater than \(1\) are \(2,3,4\); thus
\[
f(a_1)=\frac{12m}{2}+\frac{12m}{3}+\frac{12m}{4}=6m+4m+3m=13m.
\]
Now we examine the number \(13m\). If \(m\) is odd, then \(13m\) is odd; as argued, odd numbers eventually lead to a prime or prime square, so the orbit fails. If \(m\) is even but not a multiple of \(3\), then \(13m\) is even but not divisible by \(3\), and again by the previous reasoning (even not divisible by \(3\)) the orbit will eventually fail. Hence the only chance for the process to be infinite is that \(m\) is a multiple of \(6\). Write \(m=6l\). Then \(a_1=12\cdot6l=72l\) and
\[
f(a_1)=13\cdot6l=78l.
\]
Now \(78l = 6\cdot13l\). For it to be a fixed point we need that its three smallest divisors are \(2,3,6\); i.e. \(78l\) should not be divisible by \(4\) or \(5\). Since \(78\) is not divisible by \(4\), \(78l\) is divisible by \(4\) iff \(l\) is even. Also \(78l\) is divisible by \(5\) iff \(5\mid l\). Therefore we must have \(l\) odd and \(5\nmid l\). In that case \(78l\) is a fixed point, so the sequence becomes constant at \(78l\) (which has at least three proper divisors because it is a multiple of \(6\)). If \(l\) is even or divisible by \(5\), then \(78l\) is not a fixed point; its orbit under \(f\) will be strictly decreasing (since it is not a fixed point) and eventually reach a number with at most two proper divisors, making the process invalid. Thus only \(a_1=72l\) with \(l\) odd and \(5\nmid l\) are admissible in this case.

\noindent\textit{Case C: } \(4\nmid 6k\) and \(5\mid 6k\). Then \(6k=30m\) for some integer \(m\) (because \(5\mid 6k\) and \(4\nmid 6k\) forces the exponent of \(2\) to be \(1\), so we can write \(6k=30m\) with \(m\) odd). The three smallest divisors are \(2,3,5\); hence
\[
f(a_1)=\frac{30m}{2}+\frac{30m}{3}+\frac{30m}{5}=15m+10m+6m=31m.
\]
Here \(31m\) is odd, and odd numbers always eventually lead to a prime or prime square. Hence the orbit fails. No admissible numbers come from this case.

\noindent\textit{Case D: } \(4\mid 6k\) and \(5\mid 6k\). Then \(6k=60m\) for some integer \(m\). The three smallest divisors are \(2,3,4\) (because \(4<5\)), so
\[
f(a_1)=\frac{60m}{2}+\frac{60m}{3}+\frac{60m}{4}=30m+20m+15m=65m.
\]
Again \(65m\) is odd, and the same reasoning shows the orbit fails. No admissible numbers.

\noindent\textbf{3. Conclusion.}
The only initial values that can start an infinite sequence (each term having at least three proper divisors) are those described in Case A and Case B, i.e.
\[
a_1 = 6k \quad\text{with } k\text{ odd and }5\nmid k,
\]
or
\[
a_1 = 72t \quad\text{with } t\text{ odd and }5\nmid t.
\]
(These two families are disjoint because if \(a_1=72t\) then \(a_1/6=12t\) is even, while in the first family \(a_1/6\) is odd.)

\[
\boxed{a_1 = 6k\ \text{with}\ k\ \text{odd and}\ 5\nmid k,\ \text{or}\ a_1 = 72t\ \text{with}\ t\ \text{odd and}\ 5\nmid t.}
\]
</solution>
