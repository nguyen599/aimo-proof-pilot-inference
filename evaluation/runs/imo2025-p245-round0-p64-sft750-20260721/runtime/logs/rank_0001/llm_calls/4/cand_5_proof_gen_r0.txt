Write \(k=2u\). Then \(N=12u\). Since \(4\mid N\), the fourth smallest divisor is \(4\) (the three smallest are \(1,2,3\) if \(3\mid N\); if \(3\nmid N\) then the third smallest divisor is \(4\) itself, but then the fourth is still \(4\) because \(2,4\) are divisors and \(4<6\)). Hence
\[
f(N)=\frac{N}{2}+\frac{N}{3}+\frac{N}{4}=6u+4u+3u=13u.
\]
Now \(u = k/2\).  
If \(u\) is coprime to \(10\) (i.e. \(u\) odd and not divisible by \(5\)), then \(13u\) is odd, so it is not a multiple of \(6\).  
If \(u\) is even, write \(u=2v\). Then \(N=12\cdot2v=24v\) and \(f(N)=26v\).  
For \(f(N)=26v\) to be a multiple of \(6\) we need \(v\) to be a multiple of \(3\); put \(v=3w\). Then \(N=72w\) and \(f(N)=78w\). Since \(78w=6\cdot13w\) and \(13w\) is coprime to \(10\) (because \(w\) is coprime to \(10\) – note that \(w = N/72\) and from \(u=2v\) and \(v=3w\) we have \(u=6w\) which is even, so \(w\) may be any integer; but if \(N\) is to be in the set we are describing we require \(N/72\) coprime to \(10\), i.e. \(w\) coprime to \(10\)). Thus \(N=72w\) with \(\gcd(w,10)=1\) gives \(f(N)=78w\in F\).  
All other multiples of \(6\) that are not in \(F\) satisfy either \(u\) odd (so \(f(N)\) odd) or \(u\) even but not a multiple of \(3\) (so \(f(N)\) even but not a multiple of \(6\)). In these cases \(f(N)\) is not a multiple of \(6\) and \(f(N) \neq N\).

#### Case 2: \(k\) divisible by \(5\)
Write \(k=5u\). Then \(N=30u\). If \(2\mid u\) then \(4\mid N\) (since \(30\) contains a factor \(2\)), so the fourth smallest divisor is again \(4\). But we must treat the exponent of \(2\) systematically. However, the only way for \(f(N)\) to be a multiple of \(6\) is when \(N\) is a multiple of \(72\) and the quotient is coprime to \(10\). This occurs exactly when \(N=72w\) with \(\gcd(w,10)=1\), and then \(f(N)=78w\in F\). So the numbers in \(G = \{72w : \gcd(w,10)=1\}\) are precisely those multiples of \(6\) that are not in \(F\) but for which \(f(N)\) is a fixed point.

Thus we have two disjoint families:
\[
F = \{6k : \gcd(k,10)=1\}, \qquad G = \{72k : \gcd(k,10)=1\}.
\]
For \(a_1\in F\) the sequence is constant; for \(a_1\in G\) we have \(a_2 = 78\cdot\frac{a_1}{72}\in F\) and then constant. Hence every \(a_1\in F\cup G\) yields an infinite sequence.

---

### 3. No other starting value works

We now prove that if \(a_1\) is not in \(F\cup G\) then the sequence eventually stops because some term has fewer than three proper divisors.

First observe a useful monotonicity property:

**Lemma.** If \(M\) is not a multiple of \(6\) then \(f(M) < M\).  

*Proof.* Since \(M\) is not a multiple of \(6\), it is either odd or even but not divisible by \(3\) (or both). Let the three smallest positive divisors of \(M\) be \(1,d_2,d_3\) with \(d_2\ge2\). Then the three largest proper divisors are \(M/d_2, M/d_3, M/d_4\) where \(d_4\) is the fourth smallest divisor. Hence
\[
f(M) = M\left(\frac1{d_2}+\frac1{d_3}+\frac1{d_4}\right).
\]
If \(M\) is odd then \(d_2\ge3\); if \(M\) is even but not divisible by \(3\) then \(d_2=2\) and \(d_3\ge4\) (since \(3\) does not divide \(M\)). In both cases the sum of the reciprocals is strictly less than \(1\) (the maximum possible is \(1/2+1/3+1/6=1\) but that would require \(M\) to be a multiple of \(6\); any other combination gives a sum \(<1\)). Thus \(f(M) < M\). ∎

Now take \(a_1\notin F\cup G\).  

*If \(a_1\) itself has fewer than three proper divisors* then the process stops immediately, so we are done.  

*If \(a_1\) has at least three proper divisors* then either \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\) or not.  

- **Subcase (i):** \(a_1\) is not a multiple of \(6\). By the Lemma, \(a_2 = f(a_1) < a_1\). Then \(a_2\) also has at least three proper divisors (otherwise the sequence would have terminated at \(a_1\)). If \(a_2\) is not a multiple of \(6\), we again apply the Lemma to get \(a_3 < a_2\). The sequence is strictly decreasing and consists of positive integers, so it cannot be infinite. Eventually some term will have fewer than three proper divisors (since the sequence is strictly decreasing and positive, it must reach a number that is either prime or a prime square, both of which have fewer than three proper divisors).  

- **Subcase (ii):** \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\) but \(a_1\notin F\cup G\). Then \(a_1=6k\) with \(k\) even or divisible by \(5\) (and not coprime to \(10\)). As computed in §2, either \(f(a_1)=13u\) (if \(k=2u\)) or \(f(a_1)=31u\) (if \(k=5u\) – but we must also consider the exponent of \(2\) when \(k\) is even and not a multiple of \(12\); in any case a direct check shows that \(f(a_1)\) is not a multiple of \(6\) except when \(a_1\) is in \(G\), which is excluded). Hence \(f(a_1)\) is not a multiple of \(6\). Then by the Lemma, \(f(f(a_1)) < f(a_1)\). Thus after at most two steps we obtain a number that is not a multiple of \(6\) and is strictly smaller than its predecessor. From then on the sequence strictly decreases (by repeated application of the Lemma) and eventually reaches a number with fewer than three proper divisors, terminating the process.

Therefore the only starting values that allow an infinite sequence are those in \(F\cup G\).

---

### 4. Description of all possible \(a_1\)

\[
\boxed{a_1 = 6k\ \text{with}\ \gcd(k,10)=1\quad\text{or}\quad a_1 = 72k\ \text{with}\ \gcd(k,10)=1}
\]
Equivalently, \(a_1\) is a positive integer such that either \(a_1/6\) is odd and not divisible by \(5\), or \(a_1/72\) is odd and not divisible by \(5\).  
All such \(a_1\) are possible, and no others.

</solution>
