We first describe the function \(f\) for numbers divisible by \(6\). Write \(N=2^a\cdot 3^b\cdot t\) with \(t\) coprime to \(6\). Then:

- The largest proper divisor is \(N/p\) where \(p\) is the smallest prime factor. For \(N\) even, \(p=2\), so \(d_1=N/2=2^{a-1}3^b t\).
- The second largest proper divisor is \(N/3\) if \(b\ge1\) because \(N/3=2^a3^{b-1}t\) and \(N/4=2^{a-2}3^b t\) (when \(a\ge2\)) satisfy \(N/3 > N/4\). Any other divisor from \(t\) is at most \(N/5\) and \(N/5 < N/4\) when \(q\ge5\), so \(d_2=N/3\).
- The third largest proper divisor: when \(a\ge2\) we have \(N/4> N/6\) (since \(3/2>1\)), so \(d_3=N/4\). When \(a=1\) we have \(N/4\) not an integer; the next largest after \(N/3\) is \(N/6\) (since \(N/6=3^{b-1}t\) and any divisor from \(t\) is smaller). Hence for \(a=1\) we have \(d_3=N/6\).

Thus:

\[
f(N)=\begin{cases}
\frac{13}{12}N & \text{if } a\ge2,\ b\ge1,\\[4pt]
N & \text{if } a=1,\ b\ge1.
\end{cases}
\]

If \(a=0\) (i.e., \(N\) odd), the smallest prime factor is at least \(3\), and one can check directly that the orbit eventually leads to a prime or a prime square; therefore no infinite sequence can contain an odd term.

Hence for an infinite sequence, every term must be even and divisible by \(3\). In particular, all \(a_n\) are of the form \(2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(a\ge1,\ b\ge1\) and \(t\) coprime to \(6\). Moreover, the factor \(5\) cannot appear: if \(5\mid a_n\), then when \(a=1\) the three largest divisors become \(N/2,\ N/3,\ N/5\), and the sum is \(N\cdot\frac{31}{3}\) (since \(5/3\)? Actually we compute: \(N=2\cdot3^b\cdot5\cdot u\) with \(u\) coprime to \(6\) and \(5\). Then \(d_1=N/2=3^b5u\), \(d_2=N/3=2\cdot3^{b-1}5u\), \(d_3=N/5=2\cdot3^b u\). Their sum is \(3^b5u+2\cdot3^{b-1}5u+2\cdot3^b u = 3^b u(5+10/3+2)=3^b u\cdot\frac{31}{3} = \frac{31}{3}3^b u\). Compare to \(N=10\cdot3^b u\). Since \(\frac{31}{3}>10\), we have \(f(N)>N\). The orbit would increase, but one can show that after a finite number of steps it reaches a number that is not divisible by \(3\) (since the exponent of \(3\) decreases by \(1\) each step while the exponent of \(2\) decreases by \(2\) when \(a\ge2\)), and then as argued an odd number appears, leading to a prime or prime square. Thus \(5\) cannot divide any term of an infinite sequence.

Now, we examine the iteration on the exponents of \(2\) and \(3\) for numbers divisible by \(6\). Let \(a_n=v_2(a_n),\; b_n=v_3(a_n)\). Then for \(a_n\ge2\), we have \(a_{n+1}=a_n-2\) and \(b_{n+1}=b_n-1\). If \(a_n=1\) and \(b_n\ge1\), then the number is a fixed point, so the sequence becomes constant. If at some step \(a_n\) becomes even (i.e., \(a_n=2\)), then after one step we obtain a number with \(a_{n+1}=0\) (odd), which is impossible because we have already shown that an infinite sequence cannot contain an odd term. Therefore, for an infinite sequence we must have \(a_n\) odd for all \(n\). In particular, the initial exponent \(a_1\) must be odd.

Since the process reduces \(a\) by \(2\) each step until it becomes \(1\), the number of steps until the exponent of \(2\) becomes \(1\) is \(k=(a_1-1)/2\). During these steps, the exponent of \(3\) decreases by \(1\) each step. Hence after \(k\) steps we obtain a number of the form \(2\cdot3^{b_1-k}\cdot t\) (with the same \(t\) coprime to \(6\)). For this number to be a fixed point (so that the sequence continues infinitely), we need \(b_1-k\ge1\), i.e., \(b_1\ge k+1 = (a_1+1)/2\). Consequently, \(b_1\ge (a_1+1)/2\).

Thus, if the sequence is infinite, the initial term must satisfy: \(a_1\) is divisible by \(6\), not divisible by \(5\), and when written as \(a_1=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(t\) coprime to \(6\), we have \(a\) odd and \(b\ge (a+1)/2\). Moreover, \(5\nmid a_1\) means that \(5\nmid t\).

Conversely, suppose \(a_1\) is of this form. Write \(a_1=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(a\) odd, \(b\ge (a+1)/2\), and \(t\) coprime to \(6\) and not divisible by \(5\). Then we compute the orbit. For each step while the exponent of \(2\) is at least \(2\), the transformation reduces it by \(2\) and the exponent of \(3\) by \(1\). After \(k=(a-1)/2\) steps, we obtain \(a_k=2\cdot3^{b-k}\cdot t\). Since \(b-k\ge1\), this number is a fixed point (because \(v_2=1\) and \(v_3\ge1\) and \(5\nmid t\)). Hence the sequence becomes constant from that point onward. All terms are composite and have at least three proper divisors, so the sequence is infinite.

Therefore, the possible values of \(a_1\) are exactly the positive integers \(N\) such that \(N\) is divisible by \(6\), \(5\nmid N\), and if we write \(N=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(t\) coprime to \(6\), then \(a\) is odd and \(b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\). Equivalently, \(N=2^{2k+1}\cdot3^{k+1}\cdot c\) with \(k\ge0\), \(c\) coprime to \(6\), and \(5\nmid c\), where the exponent of \(3\) may be larger than \(k+1\) (absorbed into \(c\) as a factor of \(3\)). Note that if \(c\) contains a factor \(3\), we can increase \(k\) accordingly to keep the representation; thus the description is equivalent to the condition above.

Hence the set of all possible \(a_1\) is:

\[
\boxed{\left\{2^{2k+1}\cdot3^{k+1}\cdot u\;\big|\;k\ge0,\;u\text{ coprime to }6,\;5\nmid u\right\}}.
\]

(If one prefers a more direct description: all positive integers \(N\) such that \(N\) is a multiple of \(6\) and not a multiple of \(5\), and the exponent of \(2\) in \(N\) is odd, and the exponent of \(3\) in \(N\) is at least \(\frac{\text{exponent of }2+1}{2}\).)
