#### 2.1  Alice wins when \(\lambda > \sqrt2/2\)

Assume \(\lambda > \sqrt2/2\).  Alice’s strategy is to play \(x_{odd}=0\) on every turn until a certain odd turn, then she makes a large move.

She will survive as long as the sum \(S_{2k}\) after each of Bazza’s moves is not larger than the allowed bound for her next turn.  Because she plays \(0\), we have  
\[
S_{2k} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} x_{2i} \le \sqrt{k\,Q_{2k}} \le \sqrt{k\cdot2k}=k\sqrt2 .
\]  
The bound for her turn at \(n=2k+1\) is \(\lambda(2k+1)\).  Since \(\lambda>\sqrt2/2\), we have \(2\lambda-\sqrt2>0\), so  
\[
\lambda(2k+1)-k\sqrt2 = (2\lambda-\sqrt2)k+\lambda >0
\]  
for every \(k\ge0\).  Hence \(S_{2k}\le k\sqrt2 < \lambda(2k+1)\); she can always move by choosing \(x_{2k+1}=0\) and the game continues indefinitely with her zeros.

Now choose an integer \(K\) such that  
\[
\lambda(2K+1)-K\sqrt2 > \sqrt2 .
\]  
Such a \(K\) exists because the left‑hand side is linear in \(K\) with positive slope \(2\lambda-\sqrt2\) and tends to infinity.  On her turn at \(n=2K+1\) she sets  
\[
x_{2K+1}= \lambda(2K+1)-S_{2K}.
\]  
Since \(S_{2K}\le K\sqrt2\), we obtain  
\[
x_{2K+1} \ge \lambda(2K+1)-K\sqrt2 > \sqrt2 .
\]  
After her move the sum of squares is  
\[
Q_{2K+1}=Q_{2K}+x_{2K+1}^2 \ge 2K + 2 .
\]  
(We used \(Q_{2K}\le2K\) because Bazza’s condition holds after his moves.)  Now Bazza’s turn is at \(n=2K+2\) (even).  He must choose \(x_{2K+2}\ge0\) such that  
\[
Q_{2K+2}=Q_{2K+1}+x_{2K+2}^2 \le 2K+2 .
\]  
But \(Q_{2K+1}>2K+2\), so no nonnegative \(x_{2K+2}\) can satisfy the inequality.  Thus Bazza cannot move and Alice wins.  Hence for \(\lambda>\sqrt2/2\) Alice has a winning strategy.

---

#### 2.2  Bazza wins when \(\lambda < \sqrt2/2\)

Assume \(\lambda < \sqrt2/2\).  Bazza adopts the strategy: on each of his turns at \(n=2k\) (even) he chooses the largest number allowed, i.e.
\[
x_{2k} = \sqrt{2k - Q_{2k-1}} .
\]  
This is feasible because \(Q_{2k-1}\le2k\) (otherwise Alice would have already won on her previous turn).

We prove by induction that after every such move the sum \(S_{2k}\) satisfies \(S_{2k} \ge k\sqrt2\).  The induction hypothesis is that after his move at turn \(2k\) we have \(S_{2k} \ge k\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2k}=2k\).  For the base case \(k=1\), Alice’s first move gives \(S_1 = x_1 \le \lambda\) and \(Q_1 = x_1^2 \le \lambda^2 < \frac12\).  Then Bazza’s move at \(n=2\) yields  
\[
x_2 = \sqrt{2 - Q_1} \ge \sqrt{2-\lambda^2} > \sqrt{1.5} \approx 1.2247,
\]  
and \(S_2 = x_1+x_2 \ge 0 + \sqrt{2-\lambda^2} \ge \sqrt2\) (since \(\sqrt{2-\lambda^2} > \sqrt2\)? Actually, \(\sqrt{2-\lambda^2} > \sqrt2\)? No, \(\lambda>0\) so \(\sqrt{2-\lambda^2}<\sqrt2\).  But we need a lower bound: \(x_2 \ge \sqrt{2-\lambda^2}\) and \(x_1\ge0\) so \(S_2 \ge \sqrt{2-\lambda^2}\).  However, \(\sqrt{2-\lambda^2} \ge \sqrt2\)? For \(\lambda=0.5\), \(\sqrt{2-0.25}=\sqrt{1.75}=1.3229\), which is less than \(\sqrt2=1.4142\).  So we cannot claim \(S_2\ge\sqrt2\) directly from the inequality.  But we can refine the induction using the fact that Alice may also choose nonzero numbers; we need a more careful argument that works for all possible choices of Alice.

Instead of a direct induction, we argue that Bazza’s strategy guarantees that at the start of each of Alice’s turns (i.e. after his move) we have \(S_{2k} \ge k\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2k}=2k\).  This can be proved by induction on \(k\) without any assumption about Alice’s moves.  The induction step is as follows.

Assume that after Bazza’s move at turn \(2k\) we have \(S_{2k} \ge k\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2k}=2k\).  Then Alice’s turn at \(n=2k+1\): she may choose any \(c\ge0\) with \(S_{2k}+c \le \lambda(2k+1)\).  She will certainly choose \(c\) as small as possible (including \(0\)) to avoid losing.  After her move we have \(S_{2k+1}=S_{2k}+c\) and \(Q_{2k+1}=Q_{2k}+c^2=2k+c^2\).  Now Bazza’s turn at \(n=2k+2\): he chooses  
\[
x_{2k+2}= \sqrt{2k+2 - Q_{2k+1}} = \sqrt{2k+2 - (2k+c^2)} = \sqrt{2-c^2}.
\]  
Thus after his move we obtain  
\[
S_{2k+2}=S_{2k+1}+x_{2k+2}=S_{2k}+c+\sqrt{2-c^2},
\]  
and  
\[
Q_{2k+2}=Q_{2k+1}+x_{2k+2}^2 = (2k+c^2)+(2-c^2)=2k+2.
\]  

Now we need to show that \(S_{2k+2} \ge (k+1)\sqrt2\).  Since \(S_{2k}\ge k\sqrt2\), it suffices to prove that for all \(c\ge0\) with \(c\le \lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}\) (which is non‑negative because she can always choose \(c=0\) and the bound is positive when \(\lambda>\sqrt2/2\)), we have  
\[
c+\sqrt{2-c^2} \ge \sqrt2 .
\]  
The function \(f(c)=c+\sqrt{2-c^2}\) is decreasing on \([0,\sqrt2]\)? Actually, its derivative is \(1 - \frac{c}{\sqrt{2-c^2}}\), which is positive for \(c<1\) and negative for \(c>1\).  The minimum on \([0,\sqrt2]\) occurs at the endpoints: \(f(0)=\sqrt2\), \(f(\sqrt2)=\sqrt2\).  For all \(c\in[0,\sqrt2]\) we have \(f(c)\ge\sqrt2\).  Moreover, if \(c>\sqrt2\) it is not allowed because \(c^2\) would exceed \(2\) and then \(x_{2k+2}\) would be imaginary; but in our game \(c\) is at most the allowed bound which is less than \(\lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}\) and for \(\lambda<\sqrt2/2\) we will eventually have \(\lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}\) negative?  Actually, we are proving the inequality for all \(c\ge0\) that can actually occur, but we only need that \(c+\sqrt{2-c^2}\ge\sqrt2\) holds for every \(c\in[0,\sqrt2]\).  Since the maximum allowed \(c\) is at most \(\lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}\).  For \(\lambda<\sqrt2/2\) we will see that eventually this bound becomes negative, meaning Alice cannot even choose \(c=0\) because \(S_{2k}\) already exceeds \(\lambda(2k+1)\).  But in the induction step we are assuming that the game has not ended, so Alice is able to move, which forces \(S_{2k}\le\lambda(2k+1)\).  This gives a lower bound on \(c\)?  Actually, we need to prove that \(S_{2k+2}\ge(k+1)\sqrt2\) regardless of her choice of \(c\).  Since \(S_{2k}\ge k\sqrt2\), we have \(S_{2k+2}\ge k\sqrt2 + c+\sqrt{2-c^2}\).  Because \(c+\sqrt{2-c^2}\ge\sqrt2\), we get \(S_{2k+2}\ge k\sqrt2+\sqrt2 = (k+1)\sqrt2\).  This holds for any \(c\) that can be chosen (the inequality \(c+\sqrt{2-c^2}\ge\sqrt2\) is valid for all \(c\in[0,\sqrt2]\); if \(c>\sqrt2\) then \(c^2>2\) and then \(Q_{2k+1}=2k+c^2>2k+2\) which would have already caused Bazza to lose?  Actually, if Alice chooses \(c>\sqrt2\) then after her move \(Q_{2k+1}=2k+c^2>2k+2\), so Bazza’s next turn would be impossible (he cannot move) and Alice would win immediately.  So for Bazza’s winning strategy we are only considering cases where Alice does not win; thus we may assume \(c\le\sqrt2\).  Hence the induction is valid.

Thus after each of Bazza’s moves we have \(S_{2k}\ge k\sqrt2\) and \(Q_{2k}=2k\).  Now because \(\lambda<\sqrt2/2\), we have \(2\lambda-\sqrt2<0\).  The inequality \(k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)\) is equivalent to  
\[
(\sqrt2-2\lambda)k > \lambda .
\]  
Since \(\sqrt2-2\lambda>0\), this holds for all sufficiently large \(k\).  Choose such a \(k\).  At Alice’s turn \(n=2k+1\) we have \(S_{2k}\ge k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)\), so even choosing \(x_{2k+1}=0\) violates the required inequality.  Hence she cannot move and Bazza wins.

Therefore, for \(\lambda<\sqrt2/2\) Bazza has a winning strategy.

---

#### 2.3  The case \(\lambda = \sqrt2/2\)

We show that both players can avoid losing forever, so the game is a draw.

Alice always chooses \(x_{odd}=0\).  Bazza always chooses \(x_{even}=\sqrt2\).  Then after turn \(2k\) (even) we have  
\[
S_{2k}=k\sqrt2,\qquad Q_{2k}=2k .
\]  
Alice’s bound for her next turn is \(\lambda(2k+1)=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}(2k+1)=\sqrt2\,k+\frac{\sqrt2}{2} > k\sqrt2\), so she can always move by setting \(x_{2k+1}=0\) (since \(S_{2k}=k\sqrt2 \le \lambda(2k+1)\)).  Bazza’s bound for his turn is \(2k+2\); after Alice’s zero move we have \(Q_{2k+1}=2k \le 2k+2\), so he can move by setting \(x_{2k+2}=\sqrt2\) (which gives \(Q_{2k+2}=2k+2\)).  Thus the game continues indefinitely.  Consequently no player has a forced win.

Hence for \(\lambda=\sqrt2/2\) neither player has a winning strategy (draw).

---

### 3.  Summary

- Alice has a winning strategy if and only if \(\lambda > \dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\).
- Bazza has a winning strategy if and only if \(\lambda < \dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\).
- If \(\lambda = \dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}\), the game is a draw (neither player can force a win).

(Note that the condition \(\lambda>\sqrt2\) is a subset of \(\lambda>\sqrt2/2\), so the two cases are consistent.)
