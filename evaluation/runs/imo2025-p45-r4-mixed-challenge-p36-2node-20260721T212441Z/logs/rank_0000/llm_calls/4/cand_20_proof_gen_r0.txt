Write \(a_1=2^e\cdot3^f\cdot M\) where \(M\) is coprime to \(2,3,5\). Because \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\), we have \(e\ge1,\ f\ge1\). Also \(5\nmid a_1\) automatically holds because \(5\nmid M\) and \(5\nmid 2^e3^f\).

*Case \(e=1\).* Then the three smallest divisors are \(2,3,6\). Hence
\[
f(a_1)=\frac{a_1}{2}+\frac{a_1}{3}+\frac{a_1}{6}=a_1,
\]
so \(a_2=a_1\) and the sequence is constant. All terms have at least three proper divisors, so the sequence is infinite. Thus every \(a_1\) with \(e=1,\ f\ge1\) works.

*Case \(e\ge2\).* Then \(4\mid a_1\) (since \(e\ge2\)), and the three smallest divisors are \(2,3,4\). Therefore
\[
f(a_1)=\frac{a_1}{2}+\frac{a_1}{3}+\frac{a_1}{4}=\frac{13a_1}{12}.
\]
Thus \(a_2=13\cdot2^{e-2}\cdot3^{f-1}\cdot M\). For \(a_2\) to be a multiple of \(6\) (which is necessary for the sequence to be infinite, as argued earlier) we need \(e-2\ge1\) and \(f-1\ge1\), i.e., \(e\ge3\) and \(f\ge2\). If \(e=2\), then \(a_2\) is odd, and Lemma‚ÄĮ1 forces the sequence to eventually fail. Hence we must have \(e\ge3\).

Now iterate. Suppose at some stage we have a number of the form \(2^{e_i}3^{f_i}M_i\) with \(e_i\ge3,\ f_i\ge2\). Then again the three smallest divisors are \(2,3,4\) (because \(e_i\ge3\) implies divisibility by \(4\) and \(f_i\ge2\) guarantees divisibility by \(3\)). Hence
\[
e_{i+1}=e_i-2,\qquad f_{i+1}=f_i-1.
\]
This reduction continues until the exponent of \(2\) becomes \(1\). The number of steps required is \(\frac{e-1}{2}\) provided \(e\) is odd. If \(e\) is even, then after \(\frac{e}{2}\) steps we reach \(e=0\), i.e., an odd number, which fails. Therefore we must have \(e\) odd. During these steps we need \(f_i\ge1\) for all \(i\). The most restrictive condition is at the last step: \(f-\frac{e-1}{2}\ge1\), i.e., \(f\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\). Conversely, if \(e\) is odd and \(f\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\), then after each reduction the exponent of \(3\) remains positive, and when we reach \(e=1\) the number is of the form \(2\cdot3^{f'}\cdot M'\) with \(f'=f-\frac{e-1}{2}\ge1\). For such a number the three smallest divisors are \(2,3,6\), so it is a fixed point. Thus the sequence becomes constant and is infinite.

Therefore, the necessary and sufficient condition for an infinite sequence is that \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\), not divisible by \(5\), and if \(v_2(a_1)=e,\ v_3(a_1)=f\), then \(e\) is odd and \(f\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\). (The case \(e=1\) is included because \(\frac{e+1}{2}=1\) and \(f\ge1\).)
