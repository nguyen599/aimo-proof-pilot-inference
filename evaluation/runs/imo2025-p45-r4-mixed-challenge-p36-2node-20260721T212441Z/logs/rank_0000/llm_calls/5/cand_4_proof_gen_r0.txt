\lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} > \sqrt2 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \lambda(2k+1) - k\sqrt2 > \sqrt2
\;\Longleftrightarrow\; \lambda > \frac{(k+1)\sqrt2}{2k+1}.
\]
But for every $k\ge0$ we have $\frac{(k+1)\sqrt2}{2k+1} > \frac{\sqrt2}{2}$ (since $\frac{k+1}{2k+1} = \frac12 + \frac1{2(2k+1)} > \frac12$). Because $\lambda < \frac{\sqrt2}{2}$, the inequality never holds; therefore Alice cannot win on this turn. She will play $x_{2k+1}=0$ (any positive $x$ would only increase $S$ and make the situation even worse for her). Consequently $S_{2k+1}=S_{2k}=k\sqrt2$, $Q_{2k+1}=Q_{2k}=2k$.

Now Bazza’s turn $n=2k+2$. With $Q_{2k+1}=2k$ we have
\[
x_{2k+2} = \sqrt{(2k+2) - 2k} = \sqrt2 .
\]
Thus $S_{2k+2} = S_{2k+1} + \sqrt2 = (k+1)\sqrt2$, $Q_{2k+2}=2k+2$.

Hence the induction step is valid: after every even turn $n=2k$ we have $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2$, $Q_{2k}=2k$.

Now consider Alice’s turn $n=2k+1$. The condition for her to be able to move is $S_{2k} \le \lambda(2k+1)$, i.e.
\[
k\sqrt2 \le \lambda(2k+1) \;\Longleftrightarrow\; (2\lambda-\sqrt2)k \le \lambda .
\]
Since $2\lambda-\sqrt2 < 0$ (because $\lambda < 1/\sqrt2$), the left‑hand side is negative and decreasing as $k$ increases. Therefore there exists some $k$ such that $k\sqrt2 > \lambda(2k+1)$; at that odd turn Alice cannot choose any $x_{2k+1}\ge0$ (even $x=0$ would give $S_{2k}>\lambda(2k+1)$). Consequently Bazza wins.

If Alice ever deviates and chooses a positive $x$ at an odd turn, the sum $S$ becomes larger, so the condition $S_{2k} \le \lambda(2k+1)$ becomes even harder to satisfy, and she cannot win because the maximal allowed $x$ on that turn is at most $\lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}$, which is $\le\sqrt2$ (by the inequality we already used). Hence her move cannot produce a win; it only makes her lose earlier. Thus Bazza’s maximal strategy is a winning strategy for every $\lambda < 1/\sqrt2$.

---

### 3.  Alice’s winning strategy for $\lambda > 1/\sqrt2$

Assume $\lambda > 1/\sqrt2$. Alice adopts the following strategy:
- Play $x_1=0$.
- For each odd turn $n=2k+1$, if $\lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} > \sqrt2$, she plays $x_{2k+1} = \lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k}$; otherwise she plays $x_{2k+1}=0$.

We must show that this strategy guarantees a win.  
Because Alice plays $0$ at every odd turn as long as the budget does not exceed $\sqrt2$, Bazza’s maximal response at even turns (he will play the maximum allowed, which is optimal for him) yields
\[
S_{2k} = k\sqrt2,\qquad Q_{2k}=2k .
\]
Indeed, after $x_1=0$ we have $S_2=\sqrt2$, $Q_2=2$; then Alice plays $x_3=0$, so $S_3=\sqrt2$, $Q_3=2$; Bazza plays $x_4=\sqrt2$, giving $S_4=2\sqrt2$, $Q_4=4$, and so on.

Now at Alice’s turn $n=2k+1$, the expression for her budget is
\[
B_k := \lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} = \lambda(2k+1) - k\sqrt2 = (2\lambda-\sqrt2)k + \lambda .
\]
Since $\lambda > 1/\sqrt2$, we have $2\lambda-\sqrt2 > 0$. Hence $B_k$ grows without bound as $k$ increases. Consequently there exists some $k_0$ such that $B_{k_0} > \sqrt2$. At that turn Alice plays $x_{2k_0+1} = B_{k_0}$; because $B_{k_0} > \sqrt2$, we have
\[
Q_{2k_0+1} = Q_{2k_0} + B_{k_0}^2 = 2k_0 + B_{k_0}^2 > 2k_0 + 2 = 2k_0+2 .
\]
Now Bazza’s next turn (even $n=2k_0+2$) requires $Q_{2k_0+1} \le 2k_0+2$ to be able to choose a number $x_{2k_0+2}\ge0$, but $Q_{2k_0+1} > 2k_0+2$, so no such $x$ exists. Bazza loses, and Alice wins.

If Bazza ever chooses a smaller $x$ at an even turn, the sum $S_{2k}$ becomes smaller, which only increases the budget $B_k$ and makes Alice’s win even faster. Therefore the described strategy is a winning strategy for Alice whenever $\lambda > 1/\sqrt2$.

---

### 4.  The case $\lambda = 1/\sqrt2$

Let $\lambda = 1/\sqrt2$.  
If Bazza plays his maximal strategy (as above), then after every even turn $n=2k$ we have $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2$ and $Q_{2k}=2k$. At Alice’s turn $n=2k+1$ we have
\[
\lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}(2k+1) - k\sqrt2 = \frac{2k+1}{\sqrt2} - k\sqrt2 = \frac{2k+1-2k}{\sqrt2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2} = \lambda .
\]
Thus $\lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} = \lambda < \sqrt2$, so the condition $\lambda(2k+1) - S_{2k} > \sqrt2$ never holds. Alice can always move by setting $x_{2k+1}=0$ (since $S_{2k}=k\sqrt2 \le \lambda(2k+1)$). Consequently the game never ends; it is a draw.  
If Bazza deviates from the maximal strategy, Alice may win even faster (for instance, if he plays $x_2=0$, then at $n=3$ she can play $x_3=\lambda\cdot3 > \sqrt2$ and win immediately). Thus no winning strategy for Bazza exists.

For Alice, if she tries to win she would need a turn with $\lambda n - S_{n-1} > \sqrt2$. If Bazza plays maximally, $S_{n-1}=k\sqrt2$ and $\lambda(2k+1)-S_{2k}= \lambda = 1/\sqrt2 < \sqrt2$, so she cannot win. If she ever chooses a positive $x$ at an odd turn, $S$ increases and the budget becomes even smaller, so she cannot force a win. Hence Alice also has no winning strategy.

Therefore at $\lambda = 1/\sqrt2$ the game can be infinite (draw) when both players play optimally; neither player has a winning strategy.

---

### 5.  Conclusion

- Alice has a winning strategy **iff** $\lambda > \dfrac{1}{\sqrt2}$.
- Bazza has a winning strategy **iff** $\lambda < \dfrac{1}{\sqrt2}$.
- For $\lambda = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt2}$ the game can continue indefinitely (draw) and neither player has a winning strategy.

\[
\boxed{\text{Alice wins if }\lambda > \frac{1}{\sqrt2};\quad \text{Bazza wins if }\lambda < \frac{1}{\sqrt2};\quad \text{for }\lambda = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\text{ the game is a draw.}}
\]
