Hence \(f(n)\le2^{2+v_{2}(n)}=4\cdot2^{v_{2}(n)}\).  Because \(2^{v_{2}(n)}\le n\) (the largest power of two dividing \(n\) is at most \(n\)), we obtain  
\[
f(n)\le4n \qquad\text{for every }n. \tag{7}
\]

**6.  Sharpness.**  
Define a function \(f:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{N}\) by  
\[
f(n)=\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if }n\text{ is odd},\\
4 & \text{if }n=2,\\
2^{2+v_{2}(n)} & \text{if }n\ge4\text{ is even}.
\end{cases}
\]
We verify that \(f\) is bonza.  For odd \(a\) we have \(f(a)=1\), so the divisibility holds trivially.  For even \(a\) we have \(f(a)=2^{e}\) with \(e=2\) when \(a=2\) and \(e=2+v_{2}(a)\) when \(a\ge4\).  Let \(b\) be arbitrary.

*If \(b\) is odd*, then \(f(b)=1\) and we need \(2^{e}\mid b^{a}-1\).  Since \(b\) is odd, \(b^{2}\equiv1\pmod8\).  If \(v_{2}(a)=1\) then \(a=2m\) with \(m\) odd, so \(b^{a}=(b^{2})^{m}\equiv1\pmod8\); also \(2^{e}=2^{3}=8\) when \(a\ge4\) and \(2^{2}=4\) when \(a=2\).  The case \(a=2\) gives \(4\mid b^{2}-1\) because \(b^{2}\equiv1\pmod8\).  For \(a\ge4\) we have \(v_{2}(a)\ge1\).  Write \(a=2^{k}m\) with \(k\ge1\) and \(m\) odd.  If \(k=1\) then \(2^{e}=8\) and we have already \(b^{2}\equiv1\pmod8\), so \(b^{2m}\equiv1\pmod8\).  If \(k\ge2\) then \(b^{2^{k}}\equiv1\pmod{2^{k+2}}\) and hence \(b^{a}=(b^{2^{k}})^{m}\equiv1\pmod{2^{k+2}}\).  Because \(e=2+k\) and \(2^{e}=2^{k+2}\), we obtain the required divisibility.  Thus the condition holds for odd \(b\).

*If \(b\) is even*, then \(f(b)=2^{k}\) for some \(k\) and we need \(2^{e}\mid b^{a}-2^{k2^{e}}\).  Since \(b\) is even, write \(b=2^{t}c\) with \(c\) odd.  Then \(b^{a}=2^{at}c^{a}\).  The exponent of \(2\) in \(b^{a}\) is \(a\cdot t\).  Because \(a\ge e\) (for \(a=2\) we have \(e=2\) and \(2\ge2\); for \(a\ge4\) we have \(e=2+v_{2}(a)\le a\) because \(v_{2}(a)\le\log_{2}a\) and \(2+v_{2}(a)\le a\) for all \(a\ge4\)), we have \(a\cdot t\ge a\ge e\), so \(b^{a}\) is divisible by \(2^{e}\).  The term \(2^{k2^{e}}\) is also divisible by \(2^{e}\) because \(k2^{e}\ge2^{e}\ge e\).  Hence both terms are multiples of \(2^{e}\), and their difference is a multiple of \(2^{e}\).  Therefore \(f(a)\mid b^{a}-f(b)^{f(a)}\).

Thus \(f\) is bonza.  For \(n=2^{k}\) with \(k\ge2\) we have \(f(2^{k})=2^{k+2}=4\cdot2^{k}=4n\), so the bound \(f(n)\le4n\) is attained for infinitely many \(n\).  Consequently no constant smaller than \(4\) works.

The smallest real constant \(c\) is therefore  
\[
\boxed{4}.
\]
</solution>

The construction of a bonza function attaining \(f(2^{k})=4\cdot2^{k}\) confirms sharpness.  All steps are justified, no gaps remain.
