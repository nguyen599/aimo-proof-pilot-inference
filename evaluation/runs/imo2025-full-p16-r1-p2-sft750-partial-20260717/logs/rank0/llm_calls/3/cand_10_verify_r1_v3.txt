**Lemma (uniform bound).** For any bonza function \(f\) and any \(n\), we have \(f(n)\le 12n\).

*Proof.* We prove by strong induction on \(n\) that \(f(n)\le 12n\).

- Base cases: \(n=1,2,3,4,5,6\). For \(n=6\) we will show that \(f(6)\le 72\) using the conditions with \(b=2,3,5\) as done earlier.  
  The other bases are trivial because the maximum possible value is at most \(12n\) (e.g., \(f(2)\le4\), \(f(3)\le9\), \(f(4)\le16\), \(f(5)\le5\)).

- Inductive step: Assume the statement holds for all \(k<n\). We prove it for \(n\).

  **Case 1:** \(n\) is not a multiple of \(6\).  
  Then either \(n\) is odd or \(n\) is not divisible by \(3\).

  We have \(f(n)\mid (n-1)^n - f(n-1)^{f(n)}\).

  (n-1)^n - f(n-1)^{f(n)} + f(n-1)^{f(n)} = (n-1)^n.
  \]  
  This would imply \(f(n)\mid (n-1)^n\) if we also knew \(f(n)\mid f(n-1)^{f(n)}\). But we don't.  
  Instead, we can argue that from the divisibility \(f(n)\mid (n-1)^n - f(n-1)^{f(n)}\) we deduce that \(f(n)\) divides the difference of the right‑hand side and any multiple of \(f(n)\). To bound \(f(n)\), we use the condition with \(b=2\) and \(b=3\) as before.

More precisely, \(3^n - 3^{k f(n)}\) is divisible by \(3\) because both terms are multiples of \(3\) (since \(n\ge1\)), so the difference is a multiple of \(3\).
