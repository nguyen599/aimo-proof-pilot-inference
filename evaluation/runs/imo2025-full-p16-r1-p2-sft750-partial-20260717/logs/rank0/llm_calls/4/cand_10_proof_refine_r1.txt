*Case \(f(a_1)=31a_1/30\):* Then \(a_1\) is divisible by \(5\) and not by \(4\). Write \(a_1=30k\) with \(k\) odd (because the exponent of \(2\) must be \(1\)). Then \(f(a_1)=31k\). Since \(k\) is odd, \(31k\) is odd, hence not a multiple of \(2\) and therefore not a multiple of \(6\). Thus no such \(a_1\) works.

*Case \(f(a_1)=13a_1/12\):* Then \(a_1\) is divisible by \(4\). Write \(a_1=12k\). Then \(f(a_1)=13k\). For the sequence to be infinite we need \(13k\) to be a multiple of \(6\) (otherwise the next term would not be a multiple of \(6\) and the orbit would decrease). The number \(13k\) is even iff \(k\) is even, and divisible by \(3\) iff \(k\) is divisible by \(3\). Hence \(13k\) is a multiple of \(6\) exactly when \(k\) is a multiple of \(6\). Thus \(a_1=12k\) must be divisible by \(72\). Write \(a_1=72m\). Then \(f(a_1)=13\cdot6m=78m\). Now we examine \(78m\). Its three smallest divisors: \(78=2\cdot3\cdot13\), so the divisors of \(78m\) are \(2,3,6,13,\dots\). The third smallest divisor is \(6\) provided that \(m\) is not divisible by \(4\) or \(5\) (otherwise \(4\) or \(5\) would appear before \(6\)). Therefore \(78m\) is a fixed point if and only if \(m\) is odd and \(5\nmid m\). In that case the sequence becomes constant from the second term onward, and all terms have at least three proper divisors. If \(m\) is even or divisible by \(5\), then \(78m\) is not a fixed point and the orbit will eventually die (as shown in Step 1). Consequently, the only multiples of \(72\) that give an infinite orbit are those for which \(m\) is odd and \(5\nmid m\).
