## Problem
A line in the plane is called \\emph{sunny} if it is not parallel to any of the $x$-axis, the $y$-axis, or the line $x+y=0$.

Let $n \ge 3$ be a given integer. Determine all nonnegative integers $k$ such that there exist $n$ distinct lines in the plane satisfying both of the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item for all positive integers $a$ and $b$ with $a+b\le n+1$, the point $(a,b)$ lies on at least one of the lines; and
    \item exactly $k$ of the $n$ lines are sunny.
\end{itemize}

## Solution
Let \(P=\{(a,b)\in\mathbb{Z}^2\mid a\ge1,\;b\ge1,\;a+b\le n+1\}\).  \(|P|=\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\).  
A line is called *sunny* if it is not parallel to the \(x\)-axis, the \(y\)-axis or the line \(x+y=0\).  
Non‑sunny lines are therefore exactly those parallel to one of these three directions.

We are given \(n\) distinct lines, exactly \(k\) of them sunny.  The condition is that every point of \(P\) lies on at least one of the lines.  We determine all possible \(k\).

---
### 1.  Constructions for \(k=0,1,3\)

*\(k=0\)*:  Take the \(n\) horizontal lines \(y=1,2,\dots,n\).  They are all non‑sunny and cover every point of \(P\) because for each \((a,b)\in P\) we have \(b\le n\) and the point lies on the line \(y=b\).

*\(k=1\)*:  Take the \(n-1\) horizontal lines \(y=1,2,\dots,n-1\) (non‑sunny).  They cover all points with \(y\le n-1\).  The only point of \(P\) not covered is \((1,n)\).  Choose any sunny line through \((1,n)\) that is not parallel to the axes or to \(x+y=0\); for instance the line through \((1,n)\) and \((2,n-2)\) (its slope is \(-2\), hence sunny). *\(k=3\)*:  Take the \(n-3\) horizontal lines \(y=1,2,\dots,n-3\) (non‑sunny).  The remaining points of \(P\) are those with \(y\ge n-2\).  They form a triangle with corner \((1,n-2)\), \((2,n-2)\), \((1,n-1)\), \((2,n-1)\), \((1,n)\), \((3,n-2)\).  Now add three sunny lines:

L_2&:\text{ through }(1,n-1)\text{ and }(3,n-2) \quad(\text{slope }-\tfrac12),\\
L_3&:\text{ through }(1,n)\text{ and }(2,n-2) \quad(\text{slope }-2).
\end{aligned}
\]
All three slopes are different from \(0,\infty,-1\), so they are sunny.  One checks directly that these three lines together cover the six points
\[
(1,n-2),\;(2,n-2),\;(3,n-2),\;(1,n-1),\;(2,n-1),\;(1,n).
