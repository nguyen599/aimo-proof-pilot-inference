Also, because after \(r\) steps we still have a factor \(3\), the exponent \(b_0\) of \(3\) in \(N\) must satisfy \(b_0\ge r+1\) (since each step reduces the exponent of \(3\) by \(1\)).  Hence  
\[
b_0\ge \frac{a_0+1}{2}.
\]

Conversely, suppose \(N=2^{a}3^{b}c\) with \(a\) odd, \(a\ge3\), \(b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\), and \(c\) odd and not divisible by \(5\).  Write \(a=2r+1\); then \(b=r+1+t\) for some \(t\ge0\).  We have  
\[
N=2^{2r+1}3^{r+1+t}c=6\cdot12^{r}\cdot(3^{t}c).
\]  
Set \(u=3^{t}c\); then \(u\) is odd and \(5\nmid u\), so \(N=6\cdot12^{r}u\).  For \(r=0\) this is the fixed‑point case already discussed.  For \(r\ge1\) we check that the sequence is well‑defined:  
\(N\) is divisible by \(12\) and not by \(5\), hence its three smallest divisors are \(2,3,4\), so \(f(N)=13N/12=6\cdot12^{r-1}\cdot13u\).  Because \(13u\) is odd and not divisible by \(5\), the new number is again of the same form (with \(r\) decreased by \(1\)).  By induction, after \(r\) steps we obtain the fixed point \(6\cdot13^{r}u\), which has no factor \(4\) or \(5\) and therefore satisfies \(f(N)=N\).  Hence the whole orbit is constant from that point onward, so the sequence is infinite.
