* **If \(e\ge2\):** then the three smallest divisors are \(2,3,4\) (since \(4\mid N\)).  Hence  

  \[
  S(N)=\frac{N}{2}+\frac{N}{3}+\frac{N}{4}=\frac{13N}{12}.
  \]

  This is an integer because \(N\) is divisible by \(12\) (as \(e\ge2\) and \(3\mid N\)).  
  Write \(N=12K\); then \(S(N)=13K\).  For the image to be a multiple of \(3\) we need \(K\) to be a multiple of \(3\), i.e. \(f\ge2\) (since \(K=2^{e-2}3^{f-1}m\)).  If \(f=1\) then \(S(N)\) is even but not divisible by \(3\); the sequence would then be strictly decreasing and even not divisible by \(3\), eventually failing by Lemma 2.  Thus for the sequence to continue we must have \(f\ge2\).

  Now iterate: as long as the current term has exponent of \(2\) at least \(2\) and exponent of \(3\) at least \(1\), the transformation is \(N\mapsto 13N/12\).  Each such step reduces the exponent of \(2\) by \(2\) and the exponent of \(3\) by \(1\).  Therefore after \(k\) steps we obtain

  \[
  N_k=2^{e-2k}\cdot3^{f-k}\cdot m\cdot13^k.
  \]

  The process can be applied while \(e-2k\ge2\) and \(f-k\ge1\).  The number of steps until the exponent of \(2\) becomes \(1\) is \(k=\frac{e-1}{2}\) (which is an integer only if \(e\) is odd).  For the sequence to land on a fixed point we need that after this many steps the number is still divisible by \(3\) (so that it is a multiple of \(6\)) and that it is not divisible by \(5\) (otherwise the next step would give an odd term).  Thus we require  

  \[
  f-k\ge1\quad\text{and}\quad5\nmid m.
  \]

  Substituting \(k=\frac{e-1}{2}\) gives \(f\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\) and \(5\nmid m\).

  Conversely, if \(e\) is odd, \(f\ge\frac{e+1}{2}\) and \(5\nmid m\), then after \(k=\frac{e-1}{2}\) steps we reach

  \[
  N_k=2\cdot3^{f-k}\cdot m\cdot13^k.
  \]

  Because \(f-k\ge1\) we have \(3\mid N_k\), and because \(2,3,13\) are not multiples of \(5\) and \(5\nmid m\), we also have \(5\nmid N_k\).  Hence \(N_k\) is a fixed point (its three largest proper divisors sum to itself).  From that point onward the sequence is constant, so it is defined for all \(n\).

---

### 3.  Classification

We have shown that the sequence works **iff** \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\) that satisfies:

* \(v_2(a_1)\) is odd,
* \(v_3(a_1)\ge\frac{v_2(a_1)+1}{2}\),
* \(5\nmid a_1\).

In other words, \(a_1\) can be written as

\[
a_1=2^{2k+1}\cdot3^t\cdot m,\qquad k\ge0,\; t\ge k+1,\; \gcd(m,30)=1.
\]

---

### 4.  Conclusion

All possible values of \(a_1\) are the positive integers of the form \(2^{2k+1}\cdot3^t\cdot m\), where \(k\ge0\), \(t\ge k+1\), and \(m\) is any positive integer coprime to \(30\).  Equivalently, \(a_1\) is a multiple of \(6\), not divisible by \(5\), and the exponent of \(2\) in \(a_1\) is odd while the exponent of \(3\) is at least \((v_2(a_1)+1)/2\).

\[
\boxed{\text{All }a_1\text{ such that }6\mid a_1,\;5\nmid a_1,\;v_2(a_1)\text{ is odd},\;v_3(a_1)\ge\frac{v_2(a_1)+1}{2}}
\]
