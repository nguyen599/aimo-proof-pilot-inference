- \(5\) if \(a=1\) and \(5\mid t\);
- \(6\) if \(a=1\) and \(5\nmid t\) (no divisor between \(3\) and \(6\) exists).

Thus we have three cases.

**Case A:** \(a\ge2\). Then
\[
f(N)=N\!\left(\frac12+\frac13+\frac14\right)=\frac{13}{12}N=13\cdot2^{a-2}\cdot3^{b-1}\cdot t.
\]

**Case B:** \(a=1\) and \(5\mid t\). Then
\[
f(N)=N\!\left(\frac12+\frac13+\frac15\right)=\frac{31}{30}N=2\cdot31\cdot3^{b-1}\cdot\frac{t}{5}.
\]

**Case C:** \(a=1\) and \(5\nmid t\). Then
\[
f(N)=N\!\left(\frac12+\frac13+\frac16\right)=N,
\]
so \(N\) is a fixed point.

**Analysis of an infinite sequence.**  
Assume the sequence is infinite. All terms are multiples of \(6\) and therefore have \(a\ge1\), \(b\ge1\).

*Claim 1:* Every term must have the exponent of \(2\) odd.  
If some term had \(a\) even, then \(a\ge2\). If \(a=2\), Case A gives \(f(N)=13\cdot3^{b-1}\cdot t\), which is odd, contradicting Lemma 1 (odd terms cannot appear). If \(a\ge4\) and even, repeated application of Case A reduces \(a\) by \(2\) each step until we reach \(a=2\), and then the next term is odd, again impossible. Hence all terms have odd \(a\).

*Claim 2:* The sequence must eventually become constant at a fixed point.  
Because every term has odd \(a\), the exponent of \(2\) decreases by \(2\) each time we apply Case A (when \(a\ge3\)). After a finite number of steps we reach a term with \(a=1\). Let this term be \(N_0=2\cdot3^{b_0}\cdot t_0\) with \(b_0\ge1\) and \(t_0\) coprime to \(6\). If this term fell into Case B (i.e. \(5\mid t_0\)), then \(f(N_0)=2\cdot31\cdot3^{b_0-1}\cdot(t_0/5)\) would have \(a=1\) and \(b_0-1\). Repeating, we would eventually obtain a term with \(b=0\), which would be odd, contradicting Claim 1. Therefore the term with \(a=1\) must be in Case C, so \(5\nmid t_0\) and \(f(N_0)=N_0\). Hence the sequence stabilises at the fixed point \(N_0\).

Now we determine all possible starting numbers \(a_1\) that lead to such a fixed point.

Let \(a_1=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(a\ge1\), \(b\ge1\), \(\gcd(t,6)=1\). For the sequence to be infinite, the process described above must eventually reach a fixed point. The reduction of the exponent of \(2\) from \(a\) to \(1\) takes \(m=\frac{a-1}{2}\) steps (since \(a\) is odd). During these steps the exponent of \(3\) decreases by \(m\). Hence at the moment when \(a=1\) we have exponent \(b-m\). To keep the sequence infinite we need \(b-m\ge1\), i.e. \(b\ge m+1=\frac{a+1}{2}\). Moreover, after \(m\) steps the factor \(t\) becomes \(13^m\cdot t\). At the fixed point the number is \(2\cdot3^{b-m}\cdot13^m\cdot t\). Because it is a fixed point, Case C applies, so \(5\) does not divide this number. Since \(13^m\) is not divisible by \(5\), we must have \(5\nmid t\).

Conversely, suppose \(a_1=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot t\) with \(a\) odd, \(b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\), and \(5\nmid t\). We prove by induction on the exponent of \(2\) that the sequence is infinite. If \(a=1\), then \(5\nmid t\) and we are in Case C, so \(a_2=a_1\) and the sequence is constant, hence infinite. If \(a\ge3\), then by Case A we have \(a_2=13\cdot2^{a-2}\cdot3^{b-1}\cdot t\). Here \(a-2\) is odd and \(b-1\ge\frac{a+1}{2}-1=\frac{a-1}{2}=\frac{(a-2)+1}{2}}\). Also \(5\nmid 13\cdot2^{a-2}\cdot3^{b-1}\cdot t\) because \(5\nmid t\) and \(13\) and \(2\) are not multiples of \(5\). Thus the new number satisfies the same conditions. By induction, after a finite number of steps we reach a number with \(a=1\) and no factor \(5\), which is a fixed point. Therefore the sequence is infinite.

Consequently, the set of all possible values of \(a_1\) is precisely the set of positive integers that are multiples of \(6\) and, in their prime factorisation, have the exponent of \(2\) odd, the exponent of \(3\) at least \((\text{exponent of }2+1)/2\), and are not divisible by \(5\). Equivalently,
\[
\boxed{a_1 = 2^a\cdot3^b\cdot u,\quad a\text{ odd},\; b\ge\frac{a+1}{2},\; \gcd(u,6)=1,\; 5\nmid u.}
\]
