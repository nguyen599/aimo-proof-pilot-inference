The three smallest divisors of \(N\) that are greater than \(1\) are always \(2\) and \(3\). The third smallest depends on the structure:

- If \(a\ge2\) then \(4\mid N\), and since \(4>3\) but is the smallest divisor greater than \(3\), the three smallest are \(2,3,4\).  
  Consequently the three largest proper divisors are \(N/2,\;N/3,\;N/4\), and  
  \[
  f(N)=\frac{N}{2}+\frac{N}{3}+\frac{N}{4}= \frac{13}{12}N.
  \]

- If \(a=1\) then \(4\nmid N\). Because \(6\mid N\) we have \(6\mid N\), so \(6\) is a divisor. The next smallest divisor after \(3\) is either \(5\) (if \(5\mid N\)) or \(6\) (if \(5\nmid N\)). Hence:
  - If \(5\mid N\) then the three smallest are \(2,3,5\) and  
    \[
    f(N)=\frac{N}{2}+\frac{N}{3}+\frac{N}{5}= \frac{31}{30}N.
    \]
  - If \(5\nmid N\) then the three smallest are \(2,3,6\) and  
    \[
    f(N)=\frac{N}{2}+\frac{N}{3}+\frac{N}{6}= N.
    \]

Thus for a multiple of \(6\) we have  
\[
f(N)=\begin{cases}
\frac{13}{12}N, & \text{if }a\ge2,\\[4pt]
\frac{31}{30}N, & \text{if }a=1\text{ and }5\mid N,\\[4pt]
N, & \text{if }a=1\text{ and }5\nmid N.
\end{cases}
\]

---
### 3.  Characterisation of all possible \(a_1\)

Suppose the sequence \((a_n)\) is infinite. Then every term is a multiple of \(6\), so in particular \(a_1=6M_1\) with \(M_1\) integer and \(a_1\) has at least three proper divisors.

Consider \(a_1\). If \(a_1\) is a fixed point, then by the above we must have \(a=1\) and \(5\nmid c\), i.e.  
\[
a_1=2\cdot 3^b\cdot c,\qquad b\ge1,\ \text{all prime divisors of }c\ge7.
\]  
Such a term clearly yields a constant (hence infinite) sequence.

If \(a_1\) is **not** a fixed point, then either \(a\ge2\) or \(a=1\) with \(5\mid N\).  
The latter case gives \(a_2=\frac{31}{30}a_1\). Because \(v_2(a_2)=0\) (the factor \(31\) is odd and \(a_1\) has no factor \(2\) beyond the first power), \(a_2\) is odd and not a multiple of \(6\). Then by Lemma 1 the sequence would strictly decrease from \(a_2\) and eventually terminate, contradicting infiniteness. Hence we must have \(a\ge2\). So \(v_2(a_1)\ge2\).

Let \(v_2(a_1)=a\) and \(v_3(a_1)=b\), so that  
\[
a_1=2^a\cdot 3^b\cdot c,\qquad a\ge2,\ b\ge1,\ c\ \text{coprime to }6.
\]  
Since \(a_2=f(a_1)=\frac{13}{12}a_1\) must also be a multiple of \(6\), we need \(a-2\ge1\) and \(b-1\ge1\), i.e. \(a\ge3\) and \(b\ge2\).

Now, as long as the exponent of \(2\) in a term is at least \(2\), we have \(f(N)=\frac{13}{12}N\). Iterating we obtain  
\[
a_{k+1}=13^k\cdot 2^{a-2k}\cdot 3^{b-k}\cdot c,
\]  
provided \(a-2k\ge2\) and \(b-k\ge1\). For the sequence to remain multiples of \(6\) we must have \(b-k\ge1\) until the moment the exponent of \(2\) becomes \(1\). Therefore the number of steps we can take before \(v_2\) becomes \(1\) is \(k=\frac{a-1}{2}\) (this requires \(a\) to be odd). At that point we have  
\[
a_{k+1}=2\cdot 3^{b-\frac{a-1}{2}}}\cdot 13^{\frac{a-1}{2}}\cdot c.
\]  
For the sequence to be infinite, this term must not lead to termination. By the recurrence, if it has \(v_2=1\) and \(5\mid c\), then its next term would be odd and not a multiple of \(6\), causing a decrease and eventual termination. Hence we must have \(5\nmid c\). Since \(c\) is coprime to \(6\), all its prime factors are at least \(7\) (the only possible small prime is \(5\), which is excluded). Moreover, we need that when \(v_2\) becomes \(1\), the exponent of \(3\) is still at least \(1\):  
\[
b-\frac{a-1}{2}\ge1\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}.
\]

Thus for an infinite sequence we necessarily have  
- \(a=v_2(a_1)\) is odd,
- \(b=v_3(a_1)\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\),
- every prime divisor of \(c\) is at least \(7\) (so \(5\nmid c\) and \(c\) is coprime to \(6\)).

Conversely, assume that \(a_1\) can be written as  
\[
a_1=2^a\cdot 3^b\cdot c,
\]  
with \(a\) odd, \(b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\), and every prime divisor of \(c\) at least \(7\). Then \(b\ge1\) and \(c\) is coprime to \(6\) (since \(2\) and \(3\) do not divide \(c\)). Write \(a_1=2^a\cdot 3^b\cdot c\) with \(c\) having no factor \(5\). Because \(a\) is odd, set \(k=\frac{a-1}{2}\). Then  
\[
a_{k+1}=13^k\cdot 2\cdot 3^{b-k}\cdot c.
\]  
Since \(b-k\ge1\), this number is of the form \(2\cdot 3^p\cdot m\) with \(p\ge1\) and \(m=13^k\cdot c\). All prime factors of \(m\) are at least \(7\) (because \(13\ge7\) and \(c\) has primes \(\ge7\)). Hence by the recurrence for multiples of \(6\),  
\[
f(a_{k+1})=a_{k+1},
\]  
so the sequence becomes constant from that point onward. Thus the sequence is infinite.

---
### 4.  Conclusion

The possible values of \(a_1\) are exactly the positive integers that can be written as  
\[
a_1 = 2^a\cdot 3^b\cdot c,
\]  
where \(a\) is odd, \(b\ge\frac{a+1}{2}\), and every prime divisor of \(c\) is at least \(7\).

\[
\boxed{\text{All positive integers }a_1\text{ such that }a_1=2^a\cdot3^b\cdot c\text{ with }a\text{ odd, }b\ge\frac{a+1}{2},\text{ and every prime divisor of }c\ge7.}
\]
