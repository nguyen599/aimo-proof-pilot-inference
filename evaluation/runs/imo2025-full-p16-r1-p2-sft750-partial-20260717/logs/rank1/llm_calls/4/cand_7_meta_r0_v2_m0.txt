A proper divisor of a positive integer $N$ is a positive divisor of $N$ other than $N$ itself.

## Solution

We must determine all positive integers \(N\) for which the infinite sequence defined by \(a_1=N\) and \(a_{n+1}=f(a_n)\) exists (each term has at least three proper divisors).

**Lemma 1.**  
If \(N\) has at least three proper divisors, let \(d_1<d_2<d_3\) be its three smallest divisors \(>1\). Then  
\[
f(N)=N\left(\frac1{d_1}+\frac1{d_2}+\frac1{d_3}\right).
\]  
Moreover, \(\frac1{d_1}+\frac1{d_2}+\frac1{d_3}\le 1\) with equality iff \((d_1,d_2,d_3)=(2,3,6)\); in that case \(f(N)=N\) and \(N\) is a *fixed point*.  
If \((d_1,d_2,d_3)\neq(2,3,6)\) then \(\frac1{d_1}+\frac1{d_2}+\frac1{d_3}<1\) and \(f(N)<N\).

*Proof.*  
The largest proper divisors are \(N/d_1,N/d_2,N/d_3\), so the sum is \(N(1/d_1+1/d_2+1/d_3)\).  
Since the three smallest divisors are at least \(2,3,6\) and the only way to have \(1/2+1/3+1/6=1\) is with the exact triple \((2,3,6)\), the inequality follows.  Equality gives a fixed point.  If the triple is different, the sum is strictly less than \(1\).  ∎

**Lemma 2.**  
If \(N\) is odd and has at least three proper divisors, then \(f(N)\) is odd and \(f(N)<N\).  
Consequently the sequence \(N,f(N),f(f(N)),\dots\) is strictly decreasing. Hence no odd number can be the first term of an infinite sequence.

*Proof.*

**Lemma 3.**  
If \(N\) is even and not divisible by \(3\), then \(f(N)\) is even and not divisible by \(3\), and \(f(N)<N\).  
Again the sequence is strictly decreasing.  The only even numbers not divisible by \(3\) that are less than \(6\) are \(2\) and \(4\); \(2\) is prime and \(4\) has only two proper divisors.  Therefore no such number can start an infinite sequence.

*Proof.*
