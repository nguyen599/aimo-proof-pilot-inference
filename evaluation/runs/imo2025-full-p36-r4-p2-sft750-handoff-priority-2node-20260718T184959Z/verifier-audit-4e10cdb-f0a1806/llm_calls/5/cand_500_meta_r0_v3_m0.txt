Alice and Bazza are playing the inekoalaty game, a two-player game whose rules depend on a positive real number $\lambda$ which is known to both players. On the $n$th turn of the game (starting with $n=1$) the following happens:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $n$ is odd, Alice chooses a nonnegative real number $x_n$ such that
\[
x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n \le \lambda n.
\]
    \item If $n$ is even, Bazza chooses a nonnegative real number $x_n$ such that
\[
x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 \le n.
\]
\end{itemize}
If a player cannot choose a suitable $x_n$, the game ends and the other player wins. If the game goes on forever, neither player wins. All chosen numbers are known to both players.
Determine all values of $\lambda$ for which Alice has a winning strategy and all those for which Bazza has a winning strategy.

## Solution
Let the sum of chosen numbers after \(n\) turns be \(A_n\) and the sum of squares be \(S_n\). The rules are:

- For odd \(n\), Alice chooses \(x_n\ge0\) with \(A_n\le \lambda n\).
- For even \(n\), Bazza chooses \(x_n\ge0\) with \(S_n\le n\).

If a player cannot choose a nonnegative number satisfying the constraint, the other player wins. The game is infinite if both can always move.

We analyze the game by considering the state after each turn.

**1. Preliminaries.**  

Alice wins if at the end of some odd turn \(n\) we have \(S_n>n+1\) (because then at the beginning of the next turn (even \(n+1\)) Bazza cannot move). Bazza wins if at the end of some even turn \(n\) we have \(A_n>\lambda (n+1)\) (because then at the beginning of the next turn (odd \(n+2\)) Alice cannot move).

We will show that the threshold is \(\lambda=1/\sqrt2\).

**2. The case \(\lambda>1/\sqrt2\).**  

Alice can force a win with the following strategy: she plays \(x_1=0\). Then on each subsequent odd turn she plays \(x=0\) until the slack \(\lambda n-A_{n-1}\) (the maximum she may add on that turn) exceeds \(\sqrt2\). After that she plays the slack. More precisely, she maintains:

- After her first move, \(A_1=0\), \(S_1=0\).

Now we check that this is legal. Since she chooses \(x_n\le\lambda n-A_{n-1}\) by construction, the sum condition is satisfied. After her move the sum becomes \(A_n=\lambda n\) (when she finally plays the max) or remains smaller otherwise. In particular, when she finally takes the max, \(A_n=\lambda n\) and the slack is exactly the amount she adds, which is \(> \sqrt2\). Then at the moment of her move, \(S_{n-1}\) is exactly the sum of squares after the previous Bazza move. Because she has been playing zero, Bazza has been able to increase his sum of squares to the index; in fact, the worst situation for her is when Bazza plays the largest possible number, which is \(\sqrt2\) on each of his turns (this maximizes \(A_{2k}\) and makes her slack as small as possible). Under this worst-case, we have \(A_{2k}=k\sqrt2\) and \(S_{2k}=2k\). Then the slack at turn \(2k+1\) is \(\lambda(2k+1)-k\sqrt2\). Since \(\lambda>1/\sqrt2\), the coefficient \(2\lambda-\sqrt2>0\), so the slack grows without bound. Hence for some \(k\) we have \(\lambda(2k+1)-k\sqrt2>\sqrt2\). At that turn, Alice plays \(x_{2k+1}=\lambda(2k+1)-A_{2k}\). Then after her move,

\[
S_{2k+1}=S_{2k}+x_{2k+1}^2=2k+(\lambda(2k+1)-A_{2k})^2>2k+2,
\]

If Bazza ever plays a number smaller than the maximum, then his \(A_{2k}\) becomes smaller and the slack becomes even larger, so Alice can win even sooner. Hence the strategy is a winning strategy for Alice for every \(\lambda>1/\sqrt2\).

We prove that Bazza has a winning strategy. He plays \(x_2=\sqrt2\) on his first move. \[
\lambda(2k+1)-a_k = \lambda(2k+1)-k\sqrt2 = (2\lambda-\sqrt2)k+\lambda.

Because \(\lambda<1/\sqrt2\), we have \(2\lambda-\sqrt2<0\). Hence this slack is a decreasing linear function of \(k\). For sufficiently large \(k\) it becomes negative. Specifically, when \(k > \lambda/(\sqrt2-2\lambda)\), the slack is negative. At that turn, Alice cannot choose any nonnegative \(x_{2k+1}\) satisfying \(A_{2k+1}\le\lambda(2k+1)\), because \(A_{2k}=a_k\) already exceeds \(\lambda(2k+1)\). Could Alice avoid this by playing a positive number earlier? Suppose she ever takes a positive \(x\) on some odd turn.
